## Overview

Both HSAs and FSAs let you pay for qualified medical expenses with pre-tax dollars — but they work very differently. An HSA is triple tax-advantaged (contributions deductible, growth tax-free, qualified withdrawals tax-free) and funds roll over indefinitely. An FSA is use-it-or-lose-it, but is available with any employer health plan, not just high-deductible plans.

This analyzer lets you enter your expected annual medical spending, income and tax bracket, and health plan type to see the after-tax cost of your healthcare in each scenario — and which account type saves you more.

## How to Use

1. **Select your health plan type** — only HDHP-enrolled individuals qualify for an HSA
2. **Enter your expected annual medical expenses** — premiums paid out-of-pocket, deductibles, copays, prescriptions
3. **Enter your marginal tax bracket** — federal + state combined
4. **Set contribution amounts** within the 2025 limits
5. **Compare the results** — after-tax healthcare cost, tax savings, and year-end balance for each account type

## Key Concepts

- 2025 HSA limits: $4,300 (self-only HDHP) / $8,550 (family HDHP)
- 2025 FSA limit: $3,300 (employer plan), $640 rollover allowed
- HSA triple tax advantage: deductible contribution + tax-free growth + tax-free withdrawal
- FSA: use-it-or-lose-it (up to $640 rollover); available without HDHP requirement
- At age 65, HSA funds can be withdrawn for any purpose (taxed as ordinary income, like a Traditional IRA)
