## Overview

The IRS audits fewer than 1% of individual returns each year, but certain return characteristics are statistically associated with higher audit rates. High income, large Schedule C losses, unusually high deductions relative to income, unreported income, home office deductions, and round numbers are among the factors that can increase scrutiny.

This tool asks about your return's key characteristics and outputs a relative risk meter — not a prediction of whether you'll be audited, but a gauge of how your return compares to IRS audit selection patterns. It also explains what each risk factor means and what documentation you should keep on hand.

## How to Use

1. **Answer each question** about your return: income level, types of income and deductions, business losses, home office, vehicle deductions, foreign accounts
2. **View your risk meter** — a visual gauge shows your relative risk level (Low / Moderate / Elevated / High)
3. **Review the factor breakdown** — each answered question shows whether it adds or reduces risk and why
4. **Read the documentation tips** — the tool suggests what records to retain for each flagged item

## Key Concepts

- DIF score: IRS uses a Discriminant Information Function to select returns for review
- High-income filers (over $200k) face higher audit rates
- Schedule C with losses: more scrutiny, especially with large home office or vehicle deductions
- Underreported income: IRS matches W-2s, 1099s, and K-1s — mismatches trigger automated notices
- Substantiation: all deductions need contemporaneous records, not just end-of-year estimates
