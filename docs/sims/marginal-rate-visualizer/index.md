## Overview

One of the most persistent myths in personal finance is that crossing into a higher tax bracket means you pay that higher rate on *all* your income. You don't. The US uses a **progressive** system: only the income *within* each bracket is taxed at that bracket's rate. The marginal rate is what you pay on the next dollar; the effective rate is what you actually pay on average across all your income.

This visualizer makes it viscerally clear. Enter your taxable income and filing status, and watch a stacked bar fill up bracket by bracket — showing exactly how much tax is owed at each rate and what your blended effective rate is.

## How to Use

1. **Select your filing status** — the bracket thresholds differ for each
2. **Enter your taxable income** — after all deductions
3. **View the bracket chart** — each segment shows the income range, tax rate, and dollar amount of tax in that bracket
4. **Read the summary** — marginal rate (top bracket you hit), effective rate (total tax ÷ taxable income), and total tax owed

## Key Concepts

- 2025 brackets (Single): 10% / 12% / 22% / 24% / 32% / 35% / 37%
- Marginal rate = the rate on your next dollar of income
- Effective rate = total tax ÷ total taxable income (always lower than marginal rate)
- The standard deduction reduces income *before* it enters the brackets
- Long-term capital gains use separate (lower) brackets and "stack on top of" ordinary income
