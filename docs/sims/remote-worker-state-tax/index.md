## Overview

Remote work has made state taxes significantly more complicated. You might live in one state, work for a company headquartered in another, and occasionally travel to a third for meetings — and all three could potentially tax some of your income. The rules depend on each state's residency and source-income rules, "convenience of the employer" doctrines, and whether a reciprocity agreement exists between states.

This explainer walks through the key scenarios: resident vs. nonresident taxation, the "convenience rule" used by New York and a handful of other states, how reciprocity agreements work, and how to claim a credit for taxes paid to another state (to prevent true double taxation).

4. **See the state breakdown** — which states have a claim on your income, approximate tax owed to each, and the credit for taxes paid to nonresident states

## Key Concepts

- Resident state taxes all income regardless of where earned
- Nonresident state taxes income sourced to that state (work performed there)
- Convenience rule: NY, CT, DE, NE, and PA may tax remote work days as if worked in-state
- Reciprocity agreements: some neighboring states agree to only tax residents in their home state
- Credit for taxes paid to other states prevents most (but not always all) double taxation
