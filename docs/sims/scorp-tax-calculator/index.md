## Overview

The biggest tax advantage of an S-Corp for self-employed individuals is the ability to split income into two buckets: a "reasonable salary" (subject to payroll taxes) and owner distributions (not subject to SE tax). A sole proprietor pays 15.3% SE tax on all net profit; an S-Corp owner only pays payroll taxes on the salary portion.

But S-Corps come with real costs: payroll administration, separate tax returns (Form 1120-S), potential state franchise fees, and additional accounting complexity. This calculator shows whether the SE tax savings at your income level outweigh the added costs — and at what income the switch becomes worthwhile.

## How to Use

1. **Enter your net self-employment income** — annual profit from your business
2. **Enter an estimated reasonable salary** — the IRS requires S-Corp owner-employees to pay themselves a market-rate wage
3. **Enter estimated S-Corp administrative costs** — payroll service, tax preparation, state fees
4. **Compare the results** — the calculator shows SE tax as a sole proprietor vs. payroll tax as an S-Corp, net savings, and the breakeven income level

## Key Concepts

- Sole proprietor SE tax: 15.3% on net profit × 0.9235 (up to Social Security wage base)
- S-Corp: payroll taxes only on the salary; distributions avoid SE/payroll tax
- 2025 Social Security wage base: $176,100
- Reasonable salary requirement: IRS can reclassify distributions as wages if salary is too low
- Typical S-Corp breakeven: $50,000–$80,000 net profit depending on state and admin costs
