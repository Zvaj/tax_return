## Introduction: The Tax Code Rewards the Informed

Most Americans leave significant money on the table every year — not because they're doing anything wrong, but because they don't know the rules. The strategies in this chapter are not loopholes in the pejorative sense. They are legal provisions written into the tax code, often deliberately, to encourage specific behaviors: saving for retirement, investing in distressed communities, supporting charities, starting businesses, and creating jobs. The IRS isn't hiding these rules. They're sitting in plain sight in the Internal Revenue Code, used every day by people who know where to look.

What separates this chapter from a basic tax primer is the practical focus on who each strategy applies to, what the actual dollar savings look like, and what can go wrong if you execute incorrectly. Each strategy has a qualifying population, a set of mechanics, and a failure mode. Understanding all three is the difference between a legal tax reduction and an audit red flag.

We'll move from retirement accounts and real estate to business structures to investments, covering all 18 concepts in a logical sequence. Some of these strategies require a CPA or attorney to implement. Others you can do yourself with the right knowledge and a little paperwork. The chapter will tell you which is which.

## Part 1: Retirement Account Strategies for High Earners

### Backdoor Roth IRA Strategy

The Roth IRA is one of the best retirement accounts in the tax code: contributions grow tax-free and withdrawals in retirement are completely untaxed. The problem is the income limit. In 2025, if your Modified Adjusted Gross Income (MAGI) exceeds $161,000 (single) or $240,000 (married filing jointly), you cannot contribute directly to a Roth IRA. The contribution phases out starting at $146,000 (single) and $230,000 (MFJ).

The **Backdoor Roth IRA** is a two-step workaround that high earners use to get money into a Roth regardless of income:

1. Make a **non-deductible contribution** to a traditional IRA — $7,000 in 2025, or $8,000 if you're 50 or older. Because you're over the income limit for deductibility, you contribute after-tax dollars and file **Form 8606** to document the non-deductible basis.
2. **Convert** that traditional IRA balance to a Roth IRA. You can do this immediately — there is no waiting period required, though some advisors suggest waiting a few weeks to avoid the appearance of a "step transaction."

When you convert, you owe ordinary income tax on any pre-tax amounts in the IRA. If the only money in your traditional IRA is the $7,000 you just contributed after-tax, and it hasn't grown materially, the conversion is essentially tax-free.

!!! warning "The Pro-Rata Rule Will Bite You If You Have Other IRA Money"
    The backdoor Roth only works cleanly if you have **zero pre-tax dollars in any traditional, SEP, or SIMPLE IRA**. If you have a $93,000 pre-tax traditional IRA and you add $7,000 in after-tax money and then convert just $7,000, the IRS does not let you pick and choose which dollars you're converting. Under the **pro-rata rule**, the $7,000 conversion is treated as coming 93% from the pre-tax pool and 7% from your after-tax contribution. You'd owe tax on $6,510 of the conversion. The fix: roll your pre-tax IRA money into your employer's 401(k) plan before doing the backdoor Roth.

**Form 8606 is mandatory.** Every year you make a non-deductible IRA contribution, file Form 8606 to track your cumulative after-tax basis. Without this paper trail, you'll end up paying tax on money you already paid tax on when you eventually take distributions.

### Mega Backdoor Roth

If the $7,000 backdoor limit feels modest against a serious savings goal, the **Mega Backdoor Roth** pushes the envelope dramatically further. This strategy allows you to contribute significantly more to a Roth account by exploiting the gap between the standard 401(k) employee deferral limit and the overall Section 415 limit for 401(k) plans.

Here is how the math works in 2025:

- Section 415 total limit: **$70,000** per year
- Standard employee deferral: $23,500 (or $31,000 if 50+)
- Employer match: varies (say $10,000)
- Gap available for after-tax contributions: $70,000 - $23,500 - $10,000 = **$36,500**

You contribute that $36,500 as **after-tax (non-Roth) contributions** to your 401(k). Then — and this is the critical piece — you convert those after-tax contributions to Roth either via an **in-plan Roth conversion** (converting within the 401(k) to its Roth account) or an **in-service withdrawal** (withdrawing the after-tax dollars while still employed and rolling them to a Roth IRA).

!!! note "Two Requirements Your Plan Must Meet"
    The Mega Backdoor Roth only works if your 401(k) plan document allows **both** of these features:

    1. **After-tax (non-Roth) contributions** — many plans don't permit these
    2. **In-service withdrawals** or **in-plan Roth conversions** — allowing you to convert before you leave the employer

    Check your Summary Plan Description (SPD) or ask your HR/benefits department. This strategy is common at larger companies and tech firms; less common at small businesses. If your plan doesn't allow it, you cannot use this strategy regardless of how much you want to.

The tax benefit: that $36,500 in after-tax contributions grows and — once converted to Roth — all future earnings come out completely tax-free in retirement. On a 20-year horizon, compounding tax-free can turn $36,500 of after-tax contributions into substantially more in retirement wealth than the same dollars in a taxable brokerage account.

## Part 2: Real Estate Owner Strategies

### Augusta Rule Section 280A

Named for the city where the Masters golf tournament is held (local homeowners rent to spectators for enormous sums), the **Augusta Rule** lives in Section 280A(g) of the Internal Revenue Code and delivers a genuinely surprising result: if you rent your personal residence to your own business for 14 or fewer days per year, **the rental income is completely tax-free to you personally** and the rent payment is **fully deductible by your business**.

The mechanics: Your S-Corp or partnership pays you fair market rent to use your home for legitimate business purposes — board meetings, strategy retreats, client events. You deposit the rental income and never report it on your tax return. The business deducts the expense as an ordinary business cost.

!!! example "Augusta Rule in Action"
    Dr. Chen runs her medical practice through an S-Corp. She holds 10 board of directors meetings at her home over the course of the year — documented with agendas, minutes, and attendance records. Comparable event space in her market rents for $1,500/day. She charges her S-Corp $15,000 in rent for those 10 days. The S-Corp deducts $15,000, reducing its taxable income (and her self-employment tax exposure). Dr. Chen receives $15,000 in rental income — which is completely excluded from her personal taxable income under Section 280A(g). Net tax benefit at a 32% marginal rate: approximately $4,800 in federal tax savings, plus state taxes.

Several requirements must be met precisely:

- Rental must not exceed **14 days per year** — day 15 blows the exclusion entirely
- You must charge **fair market rent** (document this with comparable venue pricing, not just whatever number you want)
- There must be a **legitimate business purpose** for each meeting (no "strategy retreat" that's actually a family vacation)
- An **S-Corp, partnership, or C-Corp** structure works; a sole proprietor cannot pay rent to themselves
- Keep documentation: rental agreement between you and the entity, meeting agendas and minutes, and evidence of fair market rate

### Qualified Opportunity Zone

When you sell a highly appreciated asset — stock, real estate, a business — and face a large capital gains tax bill, the **Qualified Opportunity Zone (QOZ)** program offers a structured way to defer and potentially reduce that tax.

The rules: Within **180 days** of a capital gains-generating sale, you invest the gain (not the full proceeds — just the gain) in a **Qualified Opportunity Zone Fund**. In exchange:

- The original capital gains tax is **deferred until December 31, 2026**, or until you sell your QOZ investment, whichever is earlier — meaning you'll owe that deferred tax when you file your 2026 return in 2027
- If you hold the QOZ investment for **10 or more years**, any appreciation in the QOZ fund itself is **completely excluded from tax** — a permanent exclusion, not just a deferral

The math on a large gain is compelling. Suppose you sold a rental property and recognized $500,000 in capital gains. Invest that $500,000 in a QOZ fund. Your tax is deferred (giving you use of those funds for 1–2 years). If the QOZ investment doubles to $1,000,000 over 10 years, that $500,000 of appreciation is completely tax-free. You will eventually owe the deferred tax on the original $500,000 gain, but the gains generated by the QOZ investment itself escape tax entirely.

!!! info "QOZ Funds — What They Actually Invest In"
    Qualified Opportunity Zone Funds must invest at least 90% of their assets in **Qualified Opportunity Zone Property** — real estate or businesses located in designated low-income census tracts certified by the Treasury Department. There are over 8,700 such zones across the US. Investment quality varies enormously: some QOZ funds hold well-located urban redevelopment projects; others are in genuinely distressed areas with real investment risk. The tax benefit doesn't guarantee investment returns.

## Part 3: Charitable Giving Strategies

### Donor Advised Fund

If you give to charity and you're not using a **Donor Advised Fund (DAF)**, you're almost certainly leaving money on the table. A DAF is a charitable account — offered by financial institutions like Fidelity Charitable, Schwab Charitable, and Vanguard Charitable — where you contribute assets, receive an immediate tax deduction, and then recommend grants to charities over time at your own pace.

The real power of a DAF is **donating appreciated assets instead of cash**:

- You contribute appreciated stock (or real estate, or mutual fund shares) that you've held more than a year
- You receive a **charitable deduction for the full fair market value** — up to 30% of your AGI for appreciated assets, with a 5-year carryforward for any excess
- You **avoid the capital gains tax** that you would have owed if you had sold the asset and then donated the cash
- The DAF sells the asset (tax-free inside the DAF) and the full proceeds are available to grant to qualified charities

| Giving Method | Deduction | Capital Gains Tax | Net to Charity |
|---|---|---|---|
| Sell stock, donate cash | FMV of cash | You pay (up to 23.8%) | FMV minus cap gains tax |
| Donate appreciated stock to DAF | Full FMV | None | Full FMV |
| Donate cash directly | Cash amount | N/A | Cash amount |

The minimum to open a Fidelity Charitable DAF is $50. You can contribute any amount above that and let it sit invested until you're ready to recommend grants. This also enables **bunching**: in years when you have large deductible events (a big income year, a property sale), you can fund the DAF heavily to exceed the standard deduction threshold, then dole out grants to your favorite charities over multiple years.

### Charitable Remainder Trust

For those with very large appreciated assets and a desire for ongoing income, the **Charitable Remainder Trust (CRT)** takes charitable giving to another level — but also to another level of complexity and cost.

Here is how a CRT works: You transfer appreciated assets (stock, real estate) into an irrevocable trust. The trust sells the assets without paying capital gains tax. The trust then pays you (or another named beneficiary) an income stream for life or a term of years — typically 5–8% of the trust's initial value annually. When you and any other income beneficiaries die (or the term ends), the remaining trust assets pass to designated charities.

Your tax benefits:
- A charitable deduction in the year you fund the trust (based on the present value of the charity's remainder interest — usually 30–60% of the assets transferred)
- No immediate capital gains tax on the appreciated assets (the trust handles the sale)
- An income stream for life or term

The tradeoff: the assets in the CRT are **gone from your estate permanently**. This is an irrevocable decision. CRTs require an attorney to draft, a trustee to administer, and ongoing accounting. They make sense for assets exceeding $500,000–$1,000,000 in value where the owner is philanthropically motivated and values income more than passing the asset to heirs.

!!! warning "CRTs Are Not DIY — Use an Attorney"
    A Charitable Remainder Trust is one of the most powerful tax tools in this chapter and also one of the most complex. Improperly drafted trusts can lose their tax-exempt status, create unintended income recognition, or fail to qualify for the charitable deduction. Do not attempt this without an experienced estate planning attorney and a CPA who understands trust taxation.

## Part 4: S-Corp Strategies for the Self-Employed

### S-Corp Election Strategy

If you're self-employed and earning meaningful net profit, the **S-Corp election** is likely the highest-impact tax strategy available to you. Here's the core problem it solves: as a sole proprietor, every dollar of net profit is subject to **self-employment (SE) tax of 15.3%** (on the first $176,100) in addition to ordinary income tax. On $100,000 of net profit, that's $15,300 in SE tax alone. The S-Corp election changes the taxation structure to limit how much of your income is subject to SE tax.

The mechanism: You file **Form 2553** with the IRS to elect S-Corp status for your LLC or corporation. Once elected, you become both an employee and a shareholder of the business. The S-Corp pays you a **W-2 salary** for your services. The remaining profit — after salary, business expenses, and payroll taxes — is distributed to you as an **S-Corp distribution**. The critical difference: S-Corp distributions are **not subject to self-employment tax**.

The S-Corp tax structure:

\[
\text{SE Tax Savings} = (\text{Net Profit} - \text{Reasonable Salary}) \times 15.3\%
\]

**Example:** You net $150,000 as a sole proprietor. Your SE tax is approximately $21,226. As an S-Corp with a $75,000 salary, you pay SE tax only on the $75,000 salary portion (≈$11,475). The $75,000 distribution has no SE tax. SE tax savings: approximately **$9,751** per year. Annual S-Corp costs (payroll service + state fees): roughly $1,000–$2,000. Net benefit: ~$7,000–$9,000.

### S-Corp Reasonable Salary

The IRS requires that S-Corp owner-employees pay themselves a **reasonable salary** — one that's commensurate with what a similarly qualified employee would earn for the same work in the same market. This rule exists precisely because of the strategy above: if you could pay yourself a $1 salary and take $199,999 as a distribution, no one would pay SE tax. The IRS will reclassify unreasonably low wages as W-2 income and assess back payroll taxes, penalties, and interest.

How to determine a reasonable salary:

- Check **Bureau of Labor Statistics (BLS) Occupational Employment data** for your occupation and geographic market
- Search job postings on LinkedIn, Indeed, or Glassdoor for comparable roles
- Document your finding in writing and keep it in your corporate records

A commonly used rule of thumb: pay yourself **40–60% of net profit** as salary, with the remainder as distributions. This is not a safe harbor — it's a starting point for analysis. A surgeon netting $800,000 who pays herself a $50,000 salary will attract IRS scrutiny. A marketing consultant netting $80,000 who pays herself $45,000 is on much safer ground.

!!! tip "Document Your Salary Decision"
    Each year, document in your corporate minutes how you determined your W-2 salary. List the sources you consulted (BLS data, comparable job postings), the figures you found, and why your chosen salary is reasonable. This paper trail is invaluable if the IRS ever questions your compensation.

### S-Corp vs Sole Proprietor Tax

The S-Corp election is not free money — it comes with administrative costs that need to exceed the tax savings for the strategy to make sense. Here is a realistic comparison:

| | Sole Proprietor | S-Corp |
|---|---|---|
| SE/Payroll tax | 15.3% on all net profit | 15.3% only on W-2 salary |
| Federal income tax | Same | Same |
| Additional costs | None | Payroll service ($500–$1,500/yr), state fees ($0–$800/yr), accountant ($500–$2,000/yr) |
| Tax forms | Schedule C + SE | Form 1120-S, W-2, payroll filings |
| Breakeven net profit | — | ~$40,000–$60,000 |
| Ideal for | Net profit under $40k | Net profit over $60k |

Below roughly $40,000 in net profit, the administrative overhead of running an S-Corp typically exceeds the SE tax savings. Above $60,000–$80,000, the savings become substantial. At $120,000 net profit, the annual tax benefit commonly ranges from $8,000–$12,000 — far exceeding costs. The crossover point depends on your specific state, salary level, and actual costs.

### Health Reimbursement Account

One often-overlooked benefit of the S-Corp structure is the ability to use a **Health Reimbursement Arrangement (HRA)** to pay health insurance premiums and medical expenses tax-free. Two types are relevant:

- **ICHRA (Individual Coverage HRA):** An S-Corp can reimburse owner-employees and other employees for individual health insurance premiums purchased on the open market. Reimbursements are tax-free to the employee (deductible to the S-Corp). This is particularly valuable for S-Corp owners who cannot participate in an employer group plan.
- **QSEHRA (Qualified Small Employer HRA):** Available to employers with fewer than 50 full-time employees who don't offer a group health plan. Allows reimbursement up to $6,350/year (single) or $12,800/year (family) in 2025 for individual premiums and qualifying medical expenses.

Without an HRA structure, an S-Corp owner-employee's health insurance premiums are included in W-2 wages (creating an income inclusion) but are deductible on the owner's personal return as a self-employed health insurance deduction. The ICHRA approach can be more tax-efficient for owners and employees alike.

## Part 5: Business Investment Strategies

### Qualified Small Business Stock

If you are investing in startups or early-stage companies — or founding one yourself — **Section 1202 of the Internal Revenue Code** offers one of the most extraordinary tax exclusions on the books. **Qualified Small Business Stock (QSBS)** can make the entire gain from selling your startup shares permanently tax-free.

### Section 1202 Exclusion

Under Section 1202, if you acquire stock in a **Qualified Small Business** and hold it for more than **5 years**, you can exclude **up to 100% of the capital gain** from federal income tax when you sell. The exclusion cap is the greater of:

- **$10 million** (lifetime per company), or
- **10 times your adjusted basis** in the stock

To qualify, the company and your investment must meet all of these requirements:

- **C-Corporation only** — S-Corps, LLCs, and partnerships do not qualify
- Stock must be **originally issued** to you (not bought in the secondary market)
- Company's **aggregate gross assets** must be $50 million or less at the time of issuance (and immediately after)
- Company must be an **active qualified trade or business** — not a service business in health, law, engineering, financial services, restaurants, hotels, or farming
- You must acquire the stock in exchange for money, property, or services

!!! example "Section 1202 Exclusion — Real Dollar Impact"
    You invest $100,000 in a seed-stage tech startup in 2020. You hold for 5 years. In 2025, you sell your stake for $2,100,000 — a $2,000,000 gain. Normally that gain would generate roughly $476,000 in federal capital gains tax (23.8% including NIIT). Under Section 1202, 100% of the $2,000,000 gain is excluded from federal income tax. Tax owed: **$0** federally. State tax treatment varies — California, for instance, does not conform to Section 1202, so California residents owe the full state capital gains tax. But the federal savings alone are extraordinary.

The 10x basis limit means the exclusion scales with investment size. Invest $500,000, and you can exclude up to $5,000,000 of gain (or $10 million, whichever is greater). Some planning strategies involve multiple family members each holding QSBS from the same issuance, multiplying the available exclusion.

## Part 6: Business Loss Management

### Net Operating Loss (NOL)

When your business deductions exceed your business income in a tax year, you have a **Net Operating Loss**. An NOL is not just a bad year to forget — it is a tax asset with real future value. The NOL reduces your taxable income in the year it occurs (often to zero or below), and the unused portion can be applied to future profitable years.

The basic calculation:

\[
\text{NOL} = \text{Business Deductions} - \text{Business Income} \quad \text{(when result is negative)}
\]

**Example:** You run a consulting firm. In 2025, you have $30,000 in revenue but $85,000 in deductible business expenses (including salaries, rent, and equipment). Your NOL is $55,000. That $55,000 doesn't just disappear — it carries forward to future years.

### NOL Carryforward Rules

The Tax Cuts and Jobs Act (TCJA) significantly changed NOL rules starting in 2018. Under current law:

- **No carryback:** With limited exceptions (farming losses can be carried back 2 years), NOLs generated in 2018 and later cannot be carried back to prior years. You cannot go back and amend a profitable prior year's return.
- **Indefinite carryforward:** Your NOL carries forward indefinitely — there's no 20-year expiration window like the old rules.
- **80% limitation:** In any future year, your NOL deduction cannot exceed **80% of your taxable income** in that year.

The 80% limitation means you cannot use NOL carryforwards to zero out your taxable income in a profitable year if the NOL is large enough. You will always owe at least 20% of your taxable income even if you have a giant NOL bank.

\[
\text{NOL Deduction in Year} = \min(\text{NOL Carryforward}, \; 0.80 \times \text{Taxable Income})
\]

**Example continued:** In 2026, your consulting firm earns $120,000 net profit. You have a $55,000 NOL carryforward. The 80% limit: $120,000 × 0.80 = $96,000. Your NOL carryforward ($55,000) is less than $96,000, so you can deduct the full $55,000. Taxable income for 2026: $120,000 - $55,000 = $65,000. The NOL is fully used.

!!! note "NOLs from Real Estate"
    Real estate owners can generate significant NOLs from depreciation and other deductions exceeding rental income. If you qualify as a Real Estate Professional (see Chapter 8), those losses are not passive and can create genuine NOLs. If you do not qualify, real estate losses remain passive and suspended — they don't create an NOL. Track this distinction carefully.

## Part 7: Real Estate Tax Acceleration Strategies

### Cost Segregation Study

When you buy a commercial or residential rental property, the IRS presumes the entire building depreciates over 27.5 years (residential) or 39 years (commercial) using straight-line depreciation. But many components of a building qualify for **shorter depreciation lives** — 5, 7, or 15 years — if properly identified and reclassified. A **cost segregation study** is an engineering analysis that does exactly that: it identifies which portions of a building qualify for accelerated depreciation.

What gets reclassified:

- **5-year property:** Carpeting, appliances, certain fixtures, decorative lighting, specialty flooring
- **7-year property:** Office furniture and fixtures attached to the building
- **15-year property:** Land improvements (parking lots, landscaping, fencing, sidewalks), outdoor lighting

**The impact is enormous.** Instead of depreciating the entire $1,000,000 building over 39 years ($25,641/year), a cost segregation study might reclassify $200,000 as 5-year property and $100,000 as 15-year property. Those components can be depreciated — or bonus depreciated — much faster, generating large deductions in the early years of ownership.

Cost segregation studies typically cost $5,000–$15,000 for a commercial property. They generally make economic sense for properties with a purchase price or improvement cost of **$500,000 or more**. The tax deferral (or permanent savings if you're in a lower bracket when the recapture happens) easily justifies the cost on larger properties.

### Bonus Depreciation 2025 Rules

**Bonus depreciation** allows you to immediately deduct a large percentage of the cost of qualified property in the year it is placed in service, rather than spreading the depreciation over its recovery period. In 2025, the bonus depreciation rate is **40%** — meaning 40% of the cost of qualifying property can be deducted immediately, with the remaining 60% depreciated on the normal MACRS schedule.

This is significantly reduced from the 100% bonus depreciation that was available in 2022, but it remains a meaningful acceleration tool. The phase-down schedule under current law:

| Year | Bonus Depreciation Rate |
|---|---|
| 2022 | 100% |
| 2023 | 80% |
| 2024 | 60% |
| 2025 | 40% |
| 2026 | 20% |
| 2027+ | 0% (unless Congress acts) |

Qualifying property includes tangible property with a MACRS recovery period of **20 years or less** (5-year, 7-year, and 15-year property), certain computer software, and qualified improvement property (QIP). Critically, bonus depreciation applies to **both new and used property** — you don't have to buy a brand-new asset to benefit.

!!! tip "Combine Cost Segregation with Bonus Depreciation"
    The real power move for real estate investors is stacking these two strategies. A cost segregation study reclassifies portions of a building from 27.5/39-year property to 5-year, 7-year, or 15-year property. Once reclassified, those components are eligible for 40% bonus depreciation in 2025. On a $2,000,000 commercial building with $400,000 reclassified as 5-year property, the 40% bonus depreciation generates a $160,000 immediate deduction — on top of the normal depreciation on the remaining $1,600,000. This can create a paper loss large enough to significantly reduce taxable income in the year of acquisition.

### Section 179 Deduction

The **Section 179 deduction** allows you to immediately expense the full cost of qualifying business property in the year you place it in service — instead of depreciating it over multiple years. In 2025, the Section 179 limit is **$1,160,000**. For every dollar of property placed in service above $2,890,000, the deduction phases out dollar-for-dollar.

Key differences between Section 179 and bonus depreciation:

- **Section 179 cannot create a loss.** If your business has $50,000 of taxable income and $200,000 of Section 179 property, you can only deduct $50,000 under Section 179 (the rest carries over). Bonus depreciation has no such restriction — it can generate a loss.
- **Section 179 is elected property by property.** You choose which assets to expense under Section 179. You can mix and match Section 179 and bonus depreciation on different assets in the same year.
- **Section 179 applies to a broader category** including off-the-shelf computer software and certain qualified real property (improvements to nonresidential buildings).

A practical approach: use Section 179 first to reduce taxable income to near zero, then apply bonus depreciation to the remaining qualifying property if you want to push into a loss.

## Part 8: Family Employment Strategies

### Hire Family Members Strategy

Paying family members for legitimate work in your business is one of the simplest and most effective income-shifting strategies available to business owners. The tax logic: you deduct the wages as a business expense, reducing your taxable income at your marginal rate (potentially 22–37%). Your family member — who may be in a much lower bracket or have no income — pays taxes (if any) at their lower rate or owes nothing at all.

**Children as employees** in a sole proprietorship or partnership (not an S-Corp or C-Corp) have a particularly attractive tax benefit:

- Children under 18 employed by their parent's sole proprietorship or partnership are **exempt from FICA (Social Security and Medicare taxes)** — saving both the employer's 7.65% and the employee's 7.65%
- In 2025, a child can earn up to the **standard deduction ($15,000)** and owe zero federal income tax on that income
- The wages must be for **real work at fair market wages** — designing your social media graphics at $25/hour is legitimate; "answering phones" at $50,000/year is not

**Example:** You own a sole proprietorship netting $180,000. You hire your 16-year-old daughter to photograph products, manage your Instagram account, and perform data entry — 15 hours per week during summer, 5 hours per week during the school year. You pay her $14,000 for the year at market rates. You deduct $14,000, reducing your taxable income and self-employment tax base. She earns $14,000, which is under the standard deduction — she owes zero income tax and zero FICA. Total family tax savings: $14,000 × (your marginal rate + SE tax rate). At a combined 37% + 14.13% effective rate, that's over $7,000 in tax savings on legitimate wages.

**Spouses** can also be employed in your business, but the FICA exemption does not apply to spouses — you must withhold and remit payroll taxes on their wages. However, employing your spouse allows them to participate in your business's retirement plan, potentially adding thousands more to tax-deferred retirement savings.

!!! warning "Documentation is Not Optional"
    The IRS scrutinizes family employment arrangements. For every family member you employ:

    - Maintain time records showing hours worked and tasks performed
    - Pay market-rate wages (document comparable pay with job listings)
    - Pay via check or ACH — not cash
    - Issue a W-2 at year-end
    - Do not pay the child and then take the money back (circular transactions destroy the deduction)

    An undocumented or obviously inflated family wage is an audit risk. A well-documented, legitimate arrangement is bulletproof.

## Key Takeaways

Every strategy in this chapter is legal, used by millions of taxpayers, and well within the IRS's own rules — but none of them are automatic. They require deliberate action, correct execution, and in many cases professional help. Here is what to remember:

- **Backdoor Roth IRA:** A two-step process — non-deductible traditional IRA contribution followed by Roth conversion — available to high earners who exceed direct Roth contribution income limits. Pro-rata rule applies if you have other pre-tax IRA funds. Form 8606 is mandatory every year.
- **Mega Backdoor Roth:** After-tax 401(k) contributions up to the $70,000 Section 415 limit, converted to Roth inside the plan or via in-service withdrawal. Requires your plan to allow both after-tax contributions and conversions.
- **Augusta Rule:** Rent your home to your business for up to 14 days per year. Income is tax-free to you; deductible by the S-Corp or partnership. Fair market rent and business purpose documentation are required.
- **Qualified Opportunity Zone:** Invest capital gains in a QOZ fund within 180 days of a sale. Defers the original gain until 2026; appreciation in the QOZ investment is tax-free if held 10+ years.
- **Donor Advised Fund:** Contribute appreciated assets for an immediate deduction at full FMV, avoid capital gains tax, and recommend grants to charities over time. The DAF pays no tax when it sells the assets.
- **Charitable Remainder Trust:** Transfer appreciated assets into an irrevocable trust, avoid immediate capital gains, receive an income stream, and get a partial charitable deduction. Complex — requires an attorney.
- **S-Corp Election:** Reduces self-employment tax by splitting net profit into W-2 salary (subject to SE tax) and S-Corp distributions (not subject to SE tax). Makes economic sense at roughly $40,000–$60,000+ in net profit. File Form 2553.
- **S-Corp Reasonable Salary:** The IRS requires your W-2 salary to reflect fair market compensation for your services. Document your salary determination with BLS data or comparable job listings. Typical rule of thumb: 40–60% of net profit.
- **S-Corp vs. Sole Proprietor:** Administrative costs of S-Corp (payroll service, state fees, accountant) typically run $1,500–$4,000/year. Break-even is around $40,000–$60,000 in net profit.
- **Health Reimbursement Account:** ICHRA allows S-Corp owners to be reimbursed tax-free for individual health insurance premiums. QSEHRA covers small employers under 50 employees.
- **Qualified Small Business Stock / Section 1202:** Up to 100% exclusion of capital gains (capped at $10 million or 10x basis) on sale of C-Corp stock held 5+ years, acquired at original issuance, with gross assets under $50M at issuance. Excludes service businesses in several fields.
- **Net Operating Loss:** When business deductions exceed income, the deficit is an NOL. Under post-TCJA rules, NOLs carry forward indefinitely but are limited to 80% of taxable income in any carryforward year. No carryback (except farming).
- **Cost Segregation Study:** An engineering analysis that reclassifies building components from 27.5/39-year life to 5, 7, or 15 years, dramatically accelerating depreciation. Worth the $5,000–$15,000 study cost on properties over $500,000.
- **Bonus Depreciation 2025:** 40% immediate deduction on qualifying property (20-year or shorter recovery period, new or used) placed in service in 2025. Phasing to 20% in 2026 and 0% in 2027 under current law.
- **Section 179:** Expense up to $1,160,000 of qualifying business property in 2025. Cannot create a loss (unlike bonus depreciation). Phase-out starts at $2,890,000 of property placed in service.
- **Hire Family Members:** Pay children and spouses legitimate wages for real work. Children under 18 in a sole proprietorship or partnership are exempt from FICA. Children earning under the $15,000 standard deduction owe zero income tax. Document everything.

## What To Do Right Now

1. **Check your Roth IRA eligibility.** Look up your 2025 MAGI. If you're above $146,000 (single) or $230,000 (MFJ), you cannot contribute directly to a Roth IRA. Set up the backdoor Roth by opening a traditional IRA, making a non-deductible contribution, and converting immediately. Confirm you have no pre-tax IRA balances first.

2. **Ask HR if your 401(k) plan allows after-tax contributions and in-plan Roth conversions.** Request the Summary Plan Description. If both are allowed, calculate the gap to the $70,000 total limit and start contributing after-tax to execute the Mega Backdoor Roth.

3. **If you own a business and hold meetings at home, calendar 10–14 business events this year.** Document each with a written agenda, meeting minutes, and attendance log. Research comparable event space rental rates in your market. Have your S-Corp or partnership pay you fair market rent and issue a rental agreement.

4. **If you sold an asset with a large capital gain in 2025, check the 180-day window for QOZ investment.** Count from the date of sale. If you're within the window, research QOZ funds and evaluate whether the deferral — and potential 10-year exclusion — makes sense for your situation.

5. **If you give to charity regularly, open a Donor Advised Fund.** Fidelity Charitable has a $50 minimum. In a high-income year, contribute appreciated stock (not cash) to the DAF in a lump sum, claim the deduction now, and recommend grants to your chosen charities over the next several years.

6. **If you are self-employed with net profit above $60,000 and you are not an S-Corp, run the numbers.** Estimate your SE tax savings using the formula: (Net Profit - Reasonable Salary) × 15.3%. Compare to estimated S-Corp costs in your state. If the savings exceed the costs, consult with a CPA about filing Form 2553. The election deadline for S-Corp status generally requires filing by March 15 of the year the election is to take effect.

7. **If you own a commercial or rental property worth more than $500,000, get a cost segregation quote.** Ask a cost segregation firm for a free preliminary analysis showing the estimated reclassification and projected tax benefit. With 40% bonus depreciation available in 2025, this year may be the most advantageous time to commission a study before the bonus rate drops further.

8. **If you have children who do legitimate work in your business, set up payroll.** Establish a simple payroll system (even Gusto or QuickBooks Payroll for one employee), pay market-rate wages via check or ACH, and issue a W-2 at year-end. Keep time logs. Do not pay more than $15,000 unless the work justifies it — stay under the standard deduction to eliminate the child's income tax liability.
