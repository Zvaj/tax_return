## Introduction: Real Estate Has Its Own Tax Universe

Owning rental property puts you in a different tax world from wage earners. You get deductions most people never see — depreciation on a building you may be watching appreciate in value, interest on a mortgage that you're building equity through, and a host of operating expenses that reduce your taxable rental income to well below what you actually collected in rent. But real estate also comes with rules that can bite you if you're not paying attention: passive activity loss limits that may defer your deductions for years, depreciation recapture that can hit you with a 25% tax rate when you sell, and a web of special rules for vacation homes, short-term rentals, and home sales.

This chapter walks through all of it in practical order — starting with the basics of what counts as rental income, moving through deductions and depreciation, then tackling the bigger-picture questions around selling property and exchanging it tax-free. Along the way, you'll meet the three tax forms that do the heavy lifting for real estate: Schedule E (rental income and expenses), Schedule D (capital gains from property sales), and Form 8949 (individual asset sales). By the end, you'll understand why a landlord can show a tax loss on paper while pocketing positive cash flow every month — and whether that loss actually reduces your tax bill.

## Part 1: Reporting Rental Income and Deductions

### Rental Income Reporting

The IRS's rule on rental income is simple: if a tenant pays you money, it's taxable in the year you receive it. That applies whether you deposit the check, cash it, or have it sent directly to your mortgage servicer. Prepaid rent — say, a tenant pays January rent in December — is taxable when received, not when the period covers. The one major exception is the **security deposit**: as long as you intend to return it and are legally required to, it is not income when collected. However, the moment you apply a security deposit to unpaid rent or damages, that amount becomes taxable income in the year you keep it.

!!! note "What Counts as Rental Income"
    - All monthly rent payments received
    - Advance rent and prepaid rent (taxable when received)
    - Security deposits that are forfeited or applied to rent/damages
    - Lease cancellation payments received from tenants
    - Services received instead of rent (taxed at fair market value)
    - Expenses paid by the tenant on your behalf (e.g., they pay the utility bill)

    **Not income:** Security deposits held in trust that you intend to return.

### Rental Expense Deductions

The flip side of rental income is a robust set of deductions. You can deduct ordinary and necessary expenses for managing, conserving, and maintaining your rental property. The key distinction that trips people up is **repairs vs. improvements**: a repair restores something to its original working condition and is deductible in full in the year you pay it; an improvement adds value, adapts the property to a new use, or extends its useful life, and must be capitalized and depreciated over time.

Here is a table of the most common rental deductions and their treatment:

| Expense | Deductible | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Mortgage interest (rental property) | Yes — fully | No $750k cap; deducted on Schedule E |
| Property taxes | Yes | State and local taxes on the rental property |
| Homeowner's / landlord's insurance | Yes | Premiums for the rental period |
| Repairs and maintenance | Yes | Must restore, not improve |
| Property management fees | Yes | If you hire a manager or management company |
| Advertising / listing fees | Yes | Craigslist, Zillow, Airbnb listing fees |
| Legal and professional fees | Yes | Lease drafting, eviction attorney, tax prep |
| Depreciation | Yes | Calculated separately; see next section |
| Capital improvements | No (directly) | Capitalized; depreciated over asset life |
| Travel to inspect/manage property | Yes | Actual expense or standard mileage |

### Rental Mortgage Interest

Rental property mortgage interest is **fully deductible** on Schedule E with no dollar-cap limitation. This is a meaningful contrast to your primary residence mortgage, where interest deductibility is capped on debt up to $750,000 (or $1,000,000 for older mortgages). On a rental property, if your mortgage balance is $1.5 million and you're paying $90,000 in interest per year, every dollar of that interest is deductible against your rental income. This makes leveraged real estate particularly tax-efficient on the income side — though depreciation recapture complicates the exit.

### Home Equity Loan Deduction

If you take out a home equity loan or line of credit (HELOC), the interest is deductible only if the loan proceeds are used to **buy, build, or substantially improve the property that secures the loan**. Using HELOC proceeds to fund vacations, pay off credit cards, or invest in the stock market? That interest is not deductible at all. Using the HELOC on your primary home to fund improvements on a separate rental property? Also not deductible as mortgage interest — though it may be deductible as a rental expense if the purpose qualifies. Keep meticulous records of how you use loan proceeds; the IRS expects you to trace the money.

!!! warning "HELOC Interest Trap"
    Many taxpayers mistakenly deduct home equity interest simply because the loan is secured by their home. The IRS requires that proceeds be traceable to buying, building, or substantially improving the **secured property**. Using HELOC funds for other purposes — even other investment properties — disqualifies the interest deduction under the mortgage interest rules.

## Part 2: Depreciation — Your Biggest Phantom Deduction

### Rental Property Depreciation

Depreciation is the tax system's acknowledgment that buildings wear out over time. Even if your property is going up in value every year, the IRS lets you deduct a portion of its cost as though it were depreciating. This is called a **non-cash deduction** — it reduces your taxable income without requiring you to write a check. For residential rental property, you depreciate the building (never the land) over 27.5 years using the straight-line method under MACRS.

### MACRS Depreciation Schedule

The **Modified Accelerated Cost Recovery System (MACRS)** is the IRS's mandated depreciation system for most property placed in service after 1986. Residential rental property uses the straight-line method over a 27.5-year recovery period, which means you deduct an equal fraction of the building's adjusted cost basis each year. Land is never depreciable — you must allocate the purchase price between land and building before calculating depreciation. A common method is to use the county assessor's ratio of land-to-building value, or to hire an appraiser.

The depreciation formula for residential rental property:

\[
\text{Annual Depreciation} = \frac{\text{Purchase Price} - \text{Land Value}}{27.5}
\]

**Example:** You buy a duplex for $385,000. The county assessment allocates $60,000 to land and $325,000 to the building.

\[
\text{Annual Depreciation} = \frac{\$385{,}000 - \$60{,}000}{27.5} = \frac{\$325{,}000}{27.5} \approx \$11{,}818 \text{ per year}
\]

That's nearly $12,000 in deductions every year — a real reduction in taxable income — even if your property is gaining $30,000 in market value annually.

!!! info "Partial-Year Rule"
    In the year you place the property in service (and the year you sell it), you don't get a full year of depreciation. Residential rental property uses the **mid-month convention**: you get a half-month of depreciation for the month you place it in service, regardless of which day of the month you close.

### Qualified Improvement Property

Not all building-related depreciation follows the 27.5-year residential schedule. **Qualified Improvement Property (QIP)** applies to interior improvements made to the interior of nonresidential (commercial) buildings after the building was first placed in service. QIP has a 15-year MACRS life and is eligible for **bonus depreciation** — meaning you can potentially deduct the entire cost in year one rather than spreading it over 15 years. If you own a commercial rental building and renovate the interior, QIP rules can dramatically accelerate your deduction. Note: structural components, elevators, escalators, and the building's internal structural framework are excluded from QIP.

### Depreciation Recapture Tax

Here is the catch with depreciation: the IRS gives with one hand and takes with the other when you sell. When you sell a rental property, you must pay tax on all the depreciation you took (or were entitled to take) over your holding period. This is called **depreciation recapture**, and it is taxed at a maximum federal rate of **25%** — higher than the 15% most taxpayers pay on long-term capital gains. The relevant tax rule is Section 1250 unrecaptured gain.

**Example continued:** You sell the duplex after 10 years. You've taken $118,180 in depreciation (\$11,818 x 10). Even if you'd qualify for 0% or 15% long-term capital gains rates on the rest of the gain, that $118,180 of recaptured depreciation is taxed at up to 25%. That's potentially a $29,545 federal tax bill from depreciation recapture alone — before any state taxes.

!!! warning "You Owe Recapture Even If You Didn't Take the Deduction"
    The IRS calculates recapture based on depreciation "allowed or allowable." If you forgot to claim depreciation for three years, you still owe recapture tax on those three years when you sell. File amended returns (Form 3115) to claim missed depreciation before you sell.

## Part 3: The Passive Activity Loss Rules

### Passive Activity Loss Rules

The IRS separates your income and losses into buckets. Rental activities are, by default, **passive activities**. The fundamental rule: passive losses can only offset passive income. You cannot use a rental property loss to offset your W-2 salary, business income, or investment income — unless you qualify for an exception. This rule was enacted in 1986 specifically to prevent high-income taxpayers from sheltering wages with real estate losses. Unused passive losses are suspended and carried forward to future years, where they can offset future passive income or are released in full when you dispose of the property in a taxable sale.

There are two main exceptions that allow rental losses to offset ordinary income:

- **The $25,000 Special Allowance:** If your Adjusted Gross Income (AGI) is under $100,000 and you actively participate in the rental activity (making management decisions like setting rents, approving tenants, authorizing repairs), you can deduct up to $25,000 of rental losses against ordinary income. This allowance phases out by 50 cents for every dollar of AGI over $100,000 and disappears entirely at $150,000 AGI.
- **Real Estate Professional Status:** If you qualify as a real estate professional (covered next), your rental activities are reclassified as active, and losses are fully deductible against all income.

The phase-out math for the $25,000 allowance:

\[
\text{Allowable Loss} = \$25{,}000 - 0.50 \times (\text{AGI} - \$100{,}000)
\]

At AGI = $130,000: Allowable loss = $25,000 - 0.50 × $30,000 = $25,000 - $15,000 = **$10,000**.
At AGI = $150,000: Allowable loss = $0. Losses are fully suspended.

### Real Estate Professional Status

The **Real Estate Professional** designation is a powerful tax status that removes the passive activity limitation entirely for rental activities. To qualify, you must meet two tests in the tax year:

1. You spend more than **750 hours** in real property trades or businesses in which you materially participate.
2. More than **50% of all the personal services you perform** during the year are in real property trades or businesses.

Real property trades or businesses include development, construction, acquisition, conversion, rental, operation, management, leasing, and brokerage. A full-time employee who also owns rentals on the side typically cannot qualify — a 2,000-hour/year job already makes it impossible to meet the 50% test unless the job itself is in real estate. Qualifying allows you to fully deduct rental losses against all income, which can produce substantial tax savings if you have large depreciation deductions.

!!! tip "Hourly Log is Non-Negotiable"
    If you claim Real Estate Professional status, keep a contemporaneous log of every hour spent on real estate activities. The IRS frequently challenges this designation, and "I think I worked about 800 hours" won't hold up in court. Use a spreadsheet, calendar entries, or property management software with timestamps.

## Part 4: Short-Term Rentals and Vacation Homes

### Short-Term Rental Tax Rules

Airbnb, VRBO, and similar platforms have created a category of rental property that the IRS treats fundamentally differently from traditional long-term rentals. The critical test is the **average rental period**. If the average rental period for your property is **7 days or fewer** (think nightly and weekly rentals), the IRS considers it an active business — not a passive rental activity. This means:

- Income and expenses go on **Schedule C** (not Schedule E)
- Losses are **not subject to passive activity limitations**
- BUT profits are subject to **self-employment (SE) tax** of 15.3% (subject to the SE tax deduction)
- You may also need to collect and remit state/local occupancy taxes

The short-term rental classification can be beneficial if you're generating losses (no passive limitation) but costly if you're profitable (SE tax on top of income tax). Many short-term rental owners do not realize they're operating a business — and that their Schedule C net income is subject to SE tax just like any other self-employment income.

!!! example "Short-Term vs. Long-Term Rental"
    Priya owns two condos. Condo A is rented to a long-term tenant on a 12-month lease — average rental period 365 days. Income and expenses go on Schedule E; losses subject to passive activity rules. Condo B is listed on Airbnb with an average stay of 3 nights. Income goes on Schedule C; losses freely offset other income but profits are subject to SE tax. Same owner, two properties, two completely different tax treatments.

### Vacation Home Tax Rules

A vacation home — a property you both rent out and use personally — gets its own special ruleset. The IRS draws two bright lines:

- **Rented fewer than 15 days per year:** The rental income is completely **tax-free** and does not need to be reported. But you also cannot deduct any rental expenses. This is the "master bedroom exception" that benefits homeowners who rent their home during a major local event (Super Bowl week, for example) — rent the place out for 10 days, pocket the income tax-free.
- **Rented 15 or more days per year:** You must report all rental income and must allocate expenses between the rental use and personal use. This is where the allocation method matters.

### Mixed Personal Rental Use

When a property is used for both personal enjoyment and rental, you must split every shared expense (mortgage interest, insurance, property taxes, utilities, depreciation) between the two uses. Two allocation methods exist:

| Method | How Expenses Are Allocated | Who Benefits |
|---|---|---|
| **IRS Method** | By total days used (rental days ÷ total days the property was used) | IRS — produces smaller rental deduction |
| **Bolton Method** | By rental days ÷ total days in the year (365) | Taxpayer — produces larger rental deduction |

**Example:** Your beach house was rented for 90 days and used personally for 30 days. Total days used = 120.

- IRS Method rental ratio: 90/120 = **75%** of shared expenses allocated to rental
- Bolton Method rental ratio: 90/365 = **24.7%** of shared expenses allocated to rental

Wait — the Bolton method produces a *smaller* rental allocation. Why is it "more favorable"? Because your personal deductions (mortgage interest and property taxes) get a larger share of those expenses when you file on Schedule A, while the rental side still picks up 100% of expenses that are purely rental-related (advertising, rental management fees). The net effect is more total deductions across both categories. Tax courts have accepted both methods; the Bolton method is generally more taxpayer-favorable in practice.

!!! note "Vacation Home Expense Ordering Rule"
    When a vacation home has mixed use, you must deduct rental expenses in a specific order:
    1. Mortgage interest and property taxes (deductible in full on Schedule A if not taken as rental)
    2. Operating expenses (insurance, utilities) — allocated by your chosen method
    3. Depreciation — allocated last; cannot create a rental loss that exceeds gross rental income

## Part 5: Selling Property

### Primary Residence Sale Exclusion

When you sell your home for a profit, the IRS offers the single most generous tax break in the entire tax code for individual taxpayers: you can **exclude up to $250,000 of gain** from taxable income (or $500,000 if you're married filing jointly). This is not a deferral like a 1031 exchange — it's a permanent exclusion. The gain simply disappears from your tax return. A couple who bought their home for $300,000 and sells for $750,000 with $450,000 of gain pays zero capital gains tax on the first $500,000 — and only owes tax (at long-term capital gains rates) on the $0 of gain above exclusion if their gain fits within the cap.

### $250K/$500K Exclusion Rules

To qualify for the exclusion, you must meet two tests:

- **Ownership test:** You owned the home for at least 2 of the last 5 years before the sale date.
- **Use test:** You used the home as your primary residence for at least 2 of the last 5 years before the sale date.

The two years do not need to be consecutive, and they do not need to overlap with each other. You can claim the exclusion once every 2 years. Additional rules:

- A reduced exclusion is available if you sell due to a job change, health reasons, or unforeseen circumstances, even if you haven't met the full 2-year tests.
- If the home was previously used as a rental, the exclusion applies only to the portion of gain that accrued during qualifying residential use. Depreciation claimed during rental periods is always recaptured at 25% and cannot be excluded.
- The exclusion applies to gains, not proceeds. You must calculate your adjusted cost basis (original purchase price + capital improvements - depreciation taken during rental periods) to determine the actual gain.

!!! example "Primary Residence Sale with Rental History"
    Marcus bought a condo for $200,000 in 2018. He rented it from 2018–2021, claiming $10,909 in depreciation. He moved in as his primary residence in 2021 and sold in 2025 for $400,000. His adjusted basis = $200,000 - $10,909 = $189,091. His total gain = $400,000 - $189,091 = $210,909. The $10,909 depreciation recapture is taxed at 25% (not excludable). The remaining $200,000 gain qualifies for the primary residence exclusion (well under $250k) and is excluded. Marcus pays recapture tax on the depreciation portion only.

### Schedule D Capital Gains

**Schedule D** is where all capital gains and losses from property sales are summarized on your tax return. When you sell real estate, stock, cryptocurrency, or virtually any capital asset, the net gain or loss flows through Schedule D. Schedule D separates short-term gains (assets held 1 year or less, taxed as ordinary income) from long-term gains (assets held more than 1 year, taxed at 0%, 15%, or 20% depending on your income). Real property held more than a year generates long-term capital gains — except that the portion attributable to depreciation recapture is carved out and taxed at up to 25% regardless of holding period.

Schedule D is not where you itemize each individual transaction. That detail lives on Form 8949, and the totals roll up to Schedule D.

### Form 8949 Asset Sales

**Form 8949** is the transaction-level detail sheet for asset sales. Every individual property sale (and stock sale, crypto sale, etc.) gets its own line on Form 8949 — listing the description, acquisition date, sale date, proceeds, cost basis, and any adjustments. The transactions are separated into Part I (short-term) and Part II (long-term), and the net totals from each Part flow to the corresponding lines on Schedule D.

For real estate sales, the details required on Form 8949 include your adjusted basis (which accounts for depreciation) and the selling expenses (agent commissions, title fees, transfer taxes). These expenses reduce your realized gain and must be carefully documented.

!!! info "Form 8949 vs. Schedule D — Division of Labor"
    Think of Form 8949 as the worksheet and Schedule D as the summary. Every property or asset sale gets reported on Form 8949 first. Form 8949 totals flow to Schedule D. Schedule D totals flow to Schedule 1, which flows to your Form 1040. For real estate, you'll also need to account for the 25% recapture portion separately using the Unrecaptured Section 1250 Gain Worksheet in the Schedule D instructions.

### Schedule E Rental Income

**Schedule E (Supplemental Income and Loss)** is the reporting form for all rental property income and expenses. Each property gets its own column — up to three properties per page, with additional pages if needed. For each property, you enter the address, fair rental days, personal use days, gross rental income, and a breakdown of deductible expenses. The bottom line of Schedule E feeds into Schedule 1 (Additional Income and Adjustments), which ultimately appears on your Form 1040.

| Schedule E Section | What Goes There |
|---|---|
| Part I, Column A/B/C | Each rental property (up to 3 per page) |
| Income | Gross rents received |
| Expenses | Advertising, auto/travel, depreciation, insurance, legal, management fees, mortgage interest, taxes, repairs, supplies, utilities |
| Depreciation | From Form 4562 (Depreciation and Amortization); enter total here |
| Total income or loss | Flows to Schedule 1, Line 5 |

## Part 6: Advanced Strategies

### 1031 Like-Kind Exchange

If you sell an investment property at a gain, you can **defer all capital gains tax** — including depreciation recapture — by reinvesting the proceeds into another "like-kind" real property through a Section 1031 exchange. "Like-kind" in real estate is broadly defined: you can exchange a residential rental for a commercial building, raw land for a shopping center, or a single-family rental for a multi-family complex. What matters is that both properties are held for investment or business use — your personal residence does not qualify.

The rules are strict on timing and execution:

- You must use a **qualified intermediary (QI)** — a neutral third party who holds the sale proceeds. You cannot touch the money.
- You have **45 days** from the sale of your relinquished property to formally identify potential replacement properties (written identification to the QI).
- You have **180 days** from the sale (or your tax return due date, whichever is earlier) to close on the replacement property.
- You must reinvest all of the net proceeds and replace the debt with equal or greater debt on the new property to defer 100% of the gain. Any cash you receive ("boot") is taxable.

The 1031 exchange defers — not forgives — the gain. Your basis in the new property is reduced by the gain you deferred, which means the deferred gain will eventually be recognized when you sell the replacement property (unless you do another 1031 exchange, or hold until death, when your heirs receive a stepped-up basis).

!!! tip "The Estate Planning Angle on 1031 Exchanges"
    Savvy real estate investors use 1031 exchanges repeatedly throughout their lifetime, deferring gains into ever-larger properties. At death, heirs receive the property at a stepped-up basis equal to fair market value, completely eliminating the deferred gain. This "swap until you drop" strategy is one of the most powerful wealth transfer techniques in the tax code.

### Installment Sale Method

When you sell property and receive payments over multiple years — rather than a lump sum at closing — you can use the **installment sale method** to spread the taxable gain across the years you receive payments. This is reported on **Form 6252 (Installment Sale Income)**. Each payment you receive is divided into three components: return of basis (not taxable), interest income (taxable as ordinary income), and gain (taxable as capital gain).

The gross profit percentage drives the gain calculation:

\[
\text{Gross Profit Percentage} = \frac{\text{Gross Profit}}{\text{Contract Price}}
\]

\[
\text{Gain in Each Year} = \text{Principal Received} \times \text{Gross Profit Percentage}
\]

**Example:** You sell a rental property for $500,000 with a $200,000 adjusted basis, receiving $100,000 down and $100,000 per year for four years. Your gross profit is $300,000, and your gross profit percentage is $300,000 / $500,000 = 60%. Each $100,000 payment generates $60,000 of taxable gain in the year received. The depreciation recapture portion must all be recognized in the year of sale (not spread out), so installment sales work best when recapture is relatively small compared to overall gain.

!!! note "Installment Sale Election"
    The installment method is the default when you receive at least one payment after the tax year of sale. You can **opt out** by reporting the entire gain in the year of sale. Opting out makes sense if you expect tax rates to rise significantly, or if you have large capital losses to offset the gain in the current year.

## Key Takeaways

Real estate taxation rewards preparation and penalizes surprises. Here are the most important ideas from this chapter:

- **All rents received are taxable** in the year collected, including prepaid rent. Security deposits are not income unless forfeited.
- **Rental expenses are broad** — mortgage interest (no cap on rental property), taxes, insurance, repairs, management fees, and depreciation all reduce your taxable rental income.
- **Depreciation is a non-cash deduction** calculated as (Purchase Price - Land Value) / 27.5 for residential rentals. You must take it while renting; you pay recapture tax when you sell.
- **MACRS depreciation for residential rental property** uses straight-line method over 27.5 years. Commercial property uses 39 years. QIP (interior improvements to nonresidential property) uses 15 years and qualifies for bonus depreciation.
- **Depreciation recapture** is taxed at a maximum of 25% under Section 1250 rules — higher than typical long-term capital gains rates. It applies even if you forgot to claim depreciation.
- **Rental losses are passive** by default. They can only offset passive income, unless your AGI is under $100,000 (then up to $25,000 can offset ordinary income, phasing out by $150,000) or you qualify as a Real Estate Professional.
- **Real Estate Professional status** requires 750+ hours in real property businesses AND that real estate accounts for more than 50% of all your work hours in the year. Detailed hourly logs are essential.
- **Short-term rentals** (average stay ≤ 7 days) are treated as active businesses — Schedule C, not Schedule E; no passive loss limits, but profits face self-employment tax.
- **Vacation homes** rented fewer than 15 days per year generate tax-free income with no deductions. Rented 15+ days, income is taxable and expenses must be allocated between rental and personal use.
- **The $250,000/$500,000 primary residence exclusion** requires owning and living in the home as your primary residence for 2 of the last 5 years. Depreciation recapture from rental periods is not excludable.
- **1031 like-kind exchanges** defer all capital gains and depreciation recapture when you reinvest in another investment property. Strict rules: qualified intermediary required, 45-day identification window, 180-day closing window.
- **Installment sales** spread gain recognition across multiple years as payments arrive. Use Form 6252. Depreciation recapture must be recognized in the year of sale regardless.
- **Schedule E** reports rental income and expenses. **Schedule D** summarizes capital gains. **Form 8949** itemizes each individual sale. All three work together for real estate transactions.

## What To Do Right Now

1. **Pull last year's Schedule E.** Confirm you claimed depreciation on all rental properties. If any year was missed, consult a tax professional about filing Form 3115 to catch up — you'll need it before you sell.

2. **Calculate your current depreciation deduction.** Use the formula (Purchase Price - Land Value) / 27.5. Check your county assessor's website for the land-to-building value ratio if you don't have it.

3. **Check your AGI against the passive loss thresholds.** If your AGI is under $100,000 and you actively manage your rental, your losses (up to $25,000) are currently deductible. If you're between $100,000 and $150,000, calculate your allowable loss using the phase-out formula. If you're over $150,000, your losses are suspended until you have passive income or sell the property.

4. **Count your real estate hours if you're trying to qualify as a Real Estate Professional.** You need 750+ hours and more than 50% of your total work time in real estate. Start keeping a contemporaneous log now if you haven't already.

5. **Review your short-term rental setup.** Calculate the average rental period across all Airbnb/VRBO bookings this year. If it's 7 days or fewer, confirm you're reporting on Schedule C and accounting for self-employment tax — not just assuming it's passive rental income.

6. **Plan for any property sale this year.** If you're selling a rental, estimate your depreciation recapture liability (total depreciation taken × 25%). If you're considering a 1031 exchange, contact a qualified intermediary before you close — the QI must be in place before settlement.

7. **Check the primary residence exclusion for your home.** Verify that your ownership and use periods satisfy the 2-of-5-year tests. If you previously rented the home, calculate your adjusted basis (reducing for depreciation claimed during rental periods) to determine whether your gain exceeds the exclusion.

8. **Document HELOC and home equity loan use.** If you have a HELOC, trace the proceeds to confirm they were used to buy, build, or substantially improve the secured property. If proceeds were used elsewhere, stop deducting the interest as mortgage interest.
