## How the US Tax System Works

The United States uses a **federal income tax** system administered by the Internal Revenue Service (IRS). Congress writes the tax laws; the IRS enforces them and collects the money. Most states layer their own income taxes on top of the federal system, but this guide focuses on federal taxes — the biggest piece of your bill.

The single most important thing to understand about the US system is that it is **progressive**. That word has a specific meaning in tax law: as your income rises, each additional dollar you earn can be taxed at a higher rate than the dollar before it. This is not a penalty on success. It is a deliberate design choice meant to ensure that people with the highest ability to pay contribute proportionally more. A progressive tax system stands in contrast to a **flat tax** (everyone pays the same percentage, regardless of income) or a **regressive tax** (like sales tax, where lower earners spend a larger share of their income paying it).

The progressivity works through **tax brackets** — income ranges that each carry a specific rate. You do not pay your top rate on every dollar you earn. You pay the lowest rate on the first slice of income, a slightly higher rate on the next slice, and so on. We will walk through this in detail in the brackets section below. For now, the foundational concept to lock in is this: the US tax system charges more on higher income, but only on the portion of income that falls into each bracket.

!!! note "The IRS Is Not Your Enemy"
    The IRS processes more than 150 million individual tax returns every year. Its job is to collect the tax the law says you owe — no more, no less. Most people who end up paying too much do so not because the IRS took extra, but because they didn't claim deductions or credits they were entitled to. This guide helps you claim everything you have coming.

## Your Income: What the IRS Counts

Before the IRS can tax you, it has to define what counts as income. The starting point is **gross income** — essentially every dollar that flows to you from any source during the year, before any deductions are applied. The IRS defines gross income broadly on purpose. If something of value came to you, the default assumption is that it's taxable unless the law specifically says otherwise.

**Taxable income** is a narrower number. It's what's left after you subtract certain adjustments and deductions from your gross income. The tax brackets are applied to your taxable income, not your gross income — which means every legitimate deduction directly reduces the amount of income on which you're taxed. Understanding the difference between gross income and taxable income is the single biggest conceptual unlock in this entire guide.

Not everything that comes to you is taxable. The IRS has carved out a specific list of **non-taxable income types** that you receive free of federal income tax. Knowing this list saves people real money every year, because some taxpayers mistakenly report income they don't have to, and others make financial decisions without realizing certain benefits are tax-free.

Common taxable income sources include:

- W-2 wages, salaries, and tips
- Freelance, consulting, or gig income (reported on 1099-NEC or 1099-K)
- Self-employment business profits
- Investment income: dividends, capital gains, interest
- Rental income from property you own
- Alimony received (for divorce agreements finalized before January 1, 2019)
- Gambling winnings, prizes, and awards
- Unemployment compensation
- Most retirement account distributions (traditional IRA, 401(k))

Common non-taxable income sources include:

- Gifts received (the giver may owe gift tax, but you don't owe income tax)
- Inheritances (in most cases)
- Life insurance death benefits paid to beneficiaries
- Child support payments received
- Workers' compensation benefits
- Employer-paid health insurance premiums
- Qualified scholarships covering tuition and required fees
- Most Social Security benefits (for lower-income recipients — higher earners pay tax on a portion)
- Roth IRA and Roth 401(k) qualified distributions

!!! tip "Side Hustle Income Is Always Taxable"
    If you sold crafts on Etsy, drove for a rideshare app, or freelanced on weekends, that income is taxable — even if you never received a 1099 form. The IRS requires you to report all income regardless of whether you got paperwork. The good news: business expenses offset that income, and Chapter 6 covers exactly how to track and deduct them.

## AGI and MAGI: The Numbers That Control Everything

Once you know your gross income, the next step is calculating your **Adjusted Gross Income (AGI)**. AGI is gross income minus a specific set of deductions called "above-the-line" deductions, because they appear above the line for standard or itemized deductions on your tax return. You can claim above-the-line deductions even if you take the standard deduction — they're not in competition with each other.

AGI matters enormously because it is the gatekeeper to dozens of tax benefits. Many deductions, credits, and retirement contribution limits are phased out or eliminated entirely once your AGI crosses certain thresholds. Your AGI appears on line 11 of Form 1040, and lenders, financial aid offices, and health insurance marketplaces all ask for it regularly. Common above-the-line deductions that reduce your gross income to get to AGI include:

- Student loan interest paid (up to $2,500, with income limits)
- Contributions to a traditional IRA (with limits based on workplace plan coverage)
- Contributions to a Health Savings Account (HSA)
- Self-employment tax deduction (half of self-employment tax you paid)
- Self-employed health insurance premiums
- Alimony paid (for pre-2019 divorce agreements)
- Educator expenses (up to $300 for K-12 teachers)

**Modified AGI (MAGI)** takes your AGI and adds back certain deductions that were subtracted to calculate it. The IRS uses MAGI instead of plain AGI to determine eligibility for several key programs — including Roth IRA contributions, the Premium Tax Credit for health insurance purchased through the marketplace, and income-based repayment thresholds for student loans. The specific items added back to get MAGI vary by program, which makes it context-dependent. The most important practical rule: if a tax benefit has an "income limit," it almost always refers to your MAGI, not your gross income.

!!! example "AGI in Action"
    Maria earns $72,000 as a nurse (W-2 wages). She contributed $3,500 to her HSA and paid $1,800 in student loan interest. Her gross income is $72,000. After subtracting the HSA contribution (\$3,500) and student loan interest (\$1,800), her AGI is $66,700. That $5,300 reduction isn't just cosmetic — it may allow her to qualify for credits she'd otherwise miss, and it directly reduces the income subject to tax.

## Filing Status: Which Category Are You?

Your **filing status** is one of five categories the IRS uses to determine your tax bracket thresholds, your standard deduction amount, and your eligibility for numerous credits and deductions. Choosing the wrong status — or not knowing you qualify for a more favorable one — can mean paying hundreds or even thousands of dollars more than you owe. Filing status is determined by your situation on December 31 of the tax year, with one exception noted below.

| Filing Status | Who Qualifies | 2025 Standard Deduction |
|---|---|---|
| **Single** | Unmarried, legally separated, or divorced as of Dec 31 | $15,000 |
| **Married Filing Jointly (MFJ)** | Married couples filing one combined return | $30,000 |
| **Married Filing Separately (MFS)** | Married but filing two separate returns | $15,000 |
| **Head of Household (HoH)** | Unmarried, paid over half the cost of a home for a qualifying person | $22,500 |
| **Qualifying Surviving Spouse** | Widow(er) with dependent child, within 2 years of spouse's death | $30,000 |

Here is what you need to know about each status:

**Single** is the default status for anyone who is unmarried, legally separated, or divorced as of December 31. It carries the narrowest brackets and the smallest standard deduction of any status except Married Filing Separately.

**Married Filing Jointly** combines both spouses' income onto one return and applies wider tax brackets and a double standard deduction. For most married couples — especially where one spouse earns significantly more than the other — MFJ produces a lower combined tax bill than filing separately. The "marriage bonus" is real and common.

**Married Filing Separately** lets married couples file independent returns, each reporting only their own income and deductions. This sounds appealing but usually results in a higher combined tax bill. It does have legitimate uses: when one spouse has significant medical expenses (since MFS may make the deduction threshold easier to clear on a lower individual income) or when spouses have complex financial situations they want to keep separate. Some income-driven student loan repayment plans also benefit from MFS.

**Head of Household** is one of the most misunderstood — and most valuable — filing statuses. To qualify, you must be unmarried on December 31, have paid more than half the cost of maintaining a home during the year, and have a qualifying person (usually a child or dependent parent) who lived with you for more than half the year. HoH gives you wider brackets than Single and a significantly larger standard deduction. Many single parents qualify and never claim it.

**Qualifying Surviving Spouse** (sometimes called Qualifying Widow(er)) is available for two tax years after the year a spouse died, provided you have a dependent child living with you and you would have been able to file jointly with your deceased spouse. It preserves the MFJ bracket widths and standard deduction during a period when income may already be strained by loss.

!!! warning "Don't Guess at Your Filing Status"
    Filing status mistakes are one of the most common IRS corrections. If you're unsure whether you qualify for Head of Household, use the IRS Interactive Tax Assistant at irs.gov — it walks you through the qualifying questions in about five minutes. The difference between Single and Head of Household can be worth $1,000 or more in lower taxes.

### Marginal vs. Effective Tax Rate — Busting the Biggest Tax Myth

Here is the myth that costs people real money: "If I get a raise and cross into a higher bracket, I'll take home less money." This is completely false, and understanding why is essential.

Your **marginal tax rate** is the rate that applies to the next dollar you earn — the top bracket your income reaches. If you're a Single filer with $60,000 in taxable income, your marginal rate is 22%. But you did not pay 22% on all $60,000. You paid 10% on the first $11,925, 12% on the income between $11,926 and $48,475, and 22% only on the income between $48,476 and $60,000.

Your **effective tax rate** is your actual average rate — total federal income tax paid divided by your total taxable income. For that $60,000 earner, the effective rate works out to roughly 13–14%, nowhere near 22%. Getting a raise that pushes you into a higher bracket only means the additional dollars in that raise are taxed at the higher rate. Your existing income is taxed exactly as it was before. You will always take home more money with a raise.

The marginal rate matters for planning decisions: it tells you how much a deduction is worth. If you're in the 22% bracket, every $1,000 of additional deduction saves you $220 in federal income tax. The effective rate tells you your true overall tax burden for comparison and budgeting.

!!! tip "The Bracket Myth Costs Real Money"
    People turn down overtime, bonuses, and freelance work because they fear "moving into a higher bracket." Since only the dollars above the bracket threshold get taxed at the higher rate, turning down extra income is never the right move. A $5,000 bonus pushes you into the 22% bracket on $2,000 of it — you still net approximately $3,560 more than you had before.

## FICA Taxes: The Other Tax on Your Paycheck

Look at a pay stub for a W-2 employee and you'll see two deductions that aren't federal income tax: **Social Security** and **Medicare**. These are FICA taxes (Federal Insurance Contributions Act), and they fund the Social Security retirement and disability program and the Medicare health insurance program. Unlike income tax, FICA taxes are calculated on gross wages — before any deductions — and at flat rates.

For 2025, the employee FICA rates are:

- **Social Security tax:** 6.2% on wages up to $176,100 (the "wage base"). Once your earnings exceed $176,100, Social Security tax stops for the year.
- **Medicare tax:** 1.45% on all wages — no wage base cap.
- **Additional Medicare Tax:** An extra 0.9% on wages above $200,000 for single filers ($250,000 for MFJ). Your employer withholds this once your wages exceed $200,000 from that employer, but the actual threshold is per-person across all income sources, so you may owe additional amounts when you file.

Your employer matches your Social Security and Medicare contributions dollar for dollar, effectively doubling the contribution rate from the economy's perspective — but you don't see or pay that employer share directly.

If you are **self-employed**, you pay both the employee and employer shares of FICA as **self-employment tax**: 15.3% on net self-employment income up to the Social Security wage base, and 2.9% above it (plus the 0.9% Additional Medicare Tax if applicable). The saving grace: you can deduct half of your self-employment tax as an above-the-line deduction, reducing your AGI.

For someone earning $65,000 in W-2 wages, FICA taxes alone add up to approximately:

- Social Security: \$65,000 × 6.2% = **$4,030**
- Medicare: \$65,000 × 1.45% = **$943**
- Total FICA withheld: **$4,973** for the year

That $4,973 is separate from and in addition to federal income tax. It helps explain why people often feel like "I'm in the 22% bracket but it feels like more" — because the effective combined burden of income tax plus FICA is meaningfully higher than the income tax rate alone.

## Putting It All Together: Calculating Your Tax Liability

**Tax liability** is the total amount of federal income tax you owe for the year — calculated from your taxable income using the bracket table. This number appears on line 24 of Form 1040. It is not the same as the amount you owe when you file (that depends on how much was already withheld from your paychecks or paid in estimated taxes). Tax liability is the gross amount owed before accounting for withholding and credits.

Here is a complete, step-by-step worked example using real numbers.

**Meet Jordan.** Jordan is 34 years old, single, works as a graphic designer at a studio (W-2 income), and did some freelance design work on evenings and weekends. Here is Jordan's financial picture for 2025:

- W-2 wages from studio job: $58,000
- Freelance income (net after business expenses): $9,400
- Bank interest income: $180
- HSA contribution (made through payroll): $3,200
- Student loan interest paid: $1,100

**Step 1: Calculate Gross Income**

\[ \$58,000 + \$9,400 + \$180 = \$67,580 \]

**Step 2: Subtract Above-the-Line Deductions to Get AGI**

Jordan's above-the-line deductions:

- HSA contribution: $3,200
- Student loan interest: $1,100
- Half of self-employment tax on $9,400 of freelance income: approximately $664

\[ \$67,580 - \$3,200 - \$1,100 - \$664 = \$62,616 \text{ AGI} \]

**Step 3: Subtract the Standard Deduction to Get Taxable Income**

Jordan is single and takes the standard deduction of $15,000.

\[ \$62,616 - \$15,000 = \$47,616 \text{ taxable income} \]

**Step 4: Apply the Tax Brackets**

With $47,616 in taxable income, Jordan falls into the 12% bracket (the income is under $48,475, the top of the 12% bracket for Single filers). Here is how the brackets stack:

- 10% on the first $11,925: \$11,925 × 10% = **$1,192.50**
- 12% on $11,926 to $47,616 (that's $35,690 of income): \$35,690 × 12% = **$4,282.80**
- Total federal income tax liability: **$5,475.30**

**Step 5: Note the Effective Rate**

\[ \text{Effective Rate} = \frac{\$5,475.30}{\$47,616} \approx 11.5\% \]

Jordan's marginal rate is 12% (the bracket the income tops out in). The effective rate is 11.5% — every dollar of income cost Jordan about 11.5 cents in federal income tax on average.

**Step 6: Add Self-Employment Tax on Freelance Income**

Jordan's $9,400 of net freelance income is also subject to self-employment tax. Net self-employment income for SE tax purposes is multiplied by 92.35% first (a technical adjustment):

\[ \$9,400 \times 92.35\% = \$8,681 \]
\[ \$8,681 \times 15.3\% = \$1,328.19 \text{ self-employment tax} \]

**Step 7: Total Federal Tax Bill**

\[ \$5,475.30 \text{ (income tax)} + \$1,328.19 \text{ (SE tax)} = \$6,803.49 \text{ total federal tax liability} \]

Jordan would then compare this total to the amount already withheld from the studio paychecks. If withholding covers the income tax portion, Jordan still owes the self-employment tax — typically paid via the tax return or quarterly estimated payments.

## Key Takeaways

Before moving to Chapter 2, make sure these eight ideas are solid:

- The US tax system is **progressive**: higher income is taxed at higher rates, but only on the portion above each bracket threshold. Every dollar you earn nets you more after-tax money than the dollar before it.
- **Gross income** is everything you receive. **Taxable income** is what's left after deductions. The difference between them is where tax planning happens.
- **AGI** is your income after above-the-line deductions and before the standard deduction. It controls eligibility for many credits, deductions, and programs.
- **MAGI** is a modified version of AGI used for specific benefit calculations — most notably Roth IRA eligibility and marketplace health insurance credits.
- Your **filing status** — Single, MFJ, MFS, Head of Household, or Qualifying Surviving Spouse — determines your bracket thresholds and standard deduction. Choosing the most favorable status you qualify for is legal and advisable.
- The **marginal tax rate** is your top bracket rate. The **effective tax rate** is your actual average. Getting a raise always increases take-home pay.
- **FICA taxes** (Social Security and Medicare) are separate from income tax and appear as additional withholding on every paycheck. Self-employed workers pay the full 15.3% but deduct half.
- **Tax liability** is the total federal income tax the law says you owe — calculated from the brackets applied to taxable income. It is not the same as what you owe on April 15, which depends on withholding and credits.
