## The Reality of IRS Audits

The word "audit" triggers more anxiety than almost any other word in personal finance. People picture interrogation rooms, aggressive agents, and financial ruin. The reality is far more mundane — and far more manageable. In 2024, the IRS audited roughly **0.4% of all individual returns**, or about 1 in every 250 filers. Most of those "audits" were letters asking about a single line item, not a visit from a federal agent.

This chapter gives you the same calm, factual briefing that a good CPA gives clients before tax season: what an audit actually is, what causes them, what to do if you get one, and how to build records now that make the whole process painless. By the end of this chapter, you'll understand audits well enough that the anxiety evaporates — replaced by a simple set of habits.

## IRS Audit Types Overview

The IRS has three ways to examine a return, and they are not equivalent. Think of them as three very different conversations on a spectrum from "mild inconvenience" to "serious legal matter."

### Correspondence Audit

A **correspondence audit** is a letter — nothing more. The IRS mails you a notice asking you to verify or explain one or two specific items on your return. This is by far the most common form: correspondence audits account for roughly **75% of all IRS audits**. You respond by mail (or sometimes through an online portal) with copies of your supporting documents. You never go to an IRS office; you never speak to an agent face-to-face.

Common triggers for a correspondence audit include a mismatch between a 1099 the IRS received and what you reported, a charitable contribution deduction that seems large for your income level, or a question about whether you qualify for a specific credit. The process is transactional: you send proof, the IRS reviews it, and the matter is either closed or escalated.

!!! tip "Correspondence Audit Playbook"
    When you receive an IRS letter, read it carefully before panicking. The notice will clearly state exactly what the IRS is questioning and the deadline to respond (usually 30–60 days). Respond only to what is asked. Enclose copies of relevant documents — never originals. Send your response via certified mail and keep the tracking receipt.

### Office Audit Procedures

An **office audit** (also called a desk audit) requires you to visit an IRS Taxpayer Assistance Center in person. The IRS schedules a specific appointment and sends you a list of the items under examination. These are more comprehensive than correspondence audits — a single deduction category such as your Schedule C business expenses or charitable contributions might be reviewed in detail.

Office audits are less common than correspondence audits but more common than field audits. They are typically triggered when the IRS wants to see documents that are difficult to evaluate by mail, or when there are several related issues on the return. You are entitled to bring a representative — a CPA, enrolled agent, or tax attorney — and for an office audit, having professional representation is worth considering seriously.

### Field Audit Procedures

A **field audit** is the audit most people picture — an IRS revenue agent visits your home, your business, or your representative's office. Field audits are rare and are reserved for the most complex situations: typically business owners with complicated books, very high-income filers, or cases where the IRS suspects something more than a math error. The agent will examine your records in detail over one or more visits.

If you receive a notice scheduling a field audit, **hire a tax professional immediately**. Do not meet with an IRS revenue agent alone. You have the legal right to representation, and exercising that right is not an admission of wrongdoing — it is the rational choice.

## Audit Trigger Red Flags

The IRS uses statistical models (called the Discriminant Inventory Function, or DIF) to score every return for audit risk. Returns that score far outside the norm for their income level get a second look. Think of it as a statistical outlier detector — your return gets flagged not because the IRS thinks you're dishonest, but because your numbers look unusual compared to everyone else at your income level.

Here are the most common patterns that elevate audit risk:

- **Large charitable deductions relative to income.** Donating 40% of your income is unusual. The IRS knows what people at your income level typically donate.
- **Consistent business losses year after year.** Losing money for 3–5 consecutive years raises the question of whether this is a legitimate business or a hobby (the "hobby loss" rules under IRC §183).
- **Very high round-number deductions.** Claiming exactly $10,000 in business meals or precisely $5,000 in vehicle expenses looks estimated, not documented.
- **Cash-intensive businesses.** Restaurants, salons, contractors, and similar businesses that handle a lot of cash are scrutinized more heavily because income underreporting is common in these industries.
- **Unreported crypto transactions.** The IRS receives 1099s from exchanges and has made cryptocurrency compliance a priority since 2019. Every sale, exchange, or use of crypto to purchase goods is a taxable event.
- **Missing 1099s.** If a client pays you $3,200 and issues you a 1099-NEC, but that income doesn't appear on your return, the IRS's automated system will catch it.
- **EITC claims.** The Earned Income Tax Credit has a high error rate, so returns claiming it are audited at a higher rate — especially at lower income levels.
- **Math errors.** Not a "red flag" in the traditional sense, but mathematical errors generate automatic notices. Tax software largely eliminates this risk.

!!! warning "The Home Office Deduction Myth"
    You may have heard that claiming a home office deduction is an automatic audit trigger. This is outdated and overstated. The IRS does scrutinize home office deductions, but if you legitimately work from home and meet the **exclusive use test** (the space is used regularly and exclusively for business), you are entitled to the deduction. Using the simplified method (\$5 per square foot, up to 300 sq ft = max \$1,500) reduces scrutiny further. The real risk is claiming a home office you don't actually use exclusively for business.

## Home Office Audit Risk

The home office deduction deserves its own section because it's both widely misunderstood and legitimately valuable for self-employed filers. The exclusive use test is the key: the space must be used **regularly and exclusively** for business. A dedicated room that also has a guest bed does not qualify. A corner of the living room where the kids do homework does not qualify. A room you use only for business — even a small one — does qualify.

There are two calculation methods:

- **Simplified method:** \$5 per square foot of dedicated business space, up to 300 square feet (maximum deduction: \$1,500/year).
- **Regular (actual expenses) method:** You calculate the percentage of your home used for business — typically by dividing the office square footage by total home square footage — and apply that percentage to your actual home expenses (rent or mortgage interest, utilities, insurance, repairs).

\[
\text{Business Use Percentage} = \frac{\text{Office Square Footage}}{\text{Total Home Square Footage}} \times 100
\]

If your office is 200 square feet and your home is 2,000 square feet, your business use percentage is 10%. You can then deduct 10% of your eligible home expenses. The simplified method is easier to document and reduces IRS scrutiny, but the actual expenses method often yields a larger deduction if your home costs are high.

## IRS Audit Response Steps

Receiving an IRS audit notice does not mean you did anything wrong. It means the IRS has a question. Your job is to answer it — no more, no less. Here is the process in order:

1. **Read the notice carefully.** Identify exactly what is being questioned and the response deadline. Do not miss the deadline; missing it limits your options significantly.
2. **Gather your documentation.** Pull together receipts, bank statements, canceled checks, or whatever supports the item in question. You need to be able to prove what you claimed.
3. **Respond only to what is asked.** This is critical. Do not volunteer information about other parts of your return. Do not send more documents than requested. Each additional document is an opportunity for the examiner to find new questions.
4. **Send copies, not originals.** The IRS can lose documents. Keep your originals; send certified copies.
5. **Send via certified mail.** Get a tracking number. This creates proof that you responded and when.
6. **Consider professional representation.** For correspondence audits covering one simple item, you may handle it yourself. For office and field audits — or any situation involving multiple years or multiple issues — hire a CPA, enrolled agent, or tax attorney.
7. **Know your appeal rights.** If you disagree with the IRS's findings, you have the right to appeal to the IRS Office of Appeals, and beyond that to Tax Court, U.S. District Court, or the Court of Federal Claims.

!!! note "You Have Rights"
    The IRS Taxpayer Bill of Rights (Publication 1) guarantees you the right to be informed, the right to quality service, the right to pay no more than the correct amount of tax, the right to challenge the IRS's position, the right to appeal, and more. These are real legal rights — not just aspirational statements. If you feel an IRS examiner is acting improperly, you can contact the Taxpayer Advocate Service (TAS) for independent assistance.

## Documentation Best Practices

The best defense against an audit is great records. "Contemporaneous records" — records created at the time of the expense, not reconstructed later from memory — carry far more weight with the IRS than a spreadsheet you made the night before sending your audit response.

Here are the core practices:

- **Keep receipts for anything over \$75.** The IRS requires written receipts for business expense deductions above this threshold. For items under \$75, a log entry is technically sufficient, but a receipt is better.
- **Maintain a mileage log.** For vehicle deductions, you need the date, destination, business purpose, and miles for each trip. Apps like MileIQ or Everlance automate this. A contemporaneous log is required — recreating it at year-end from calendar entries is permissible but weaker.
- **Use a dedicated business account.** Mixing personal and business expenses is the single biggest record-keeping mistake self-employed filers make. A separate business checking account and business credit card create a clean, auditable paper trail automatically.
- **Document the business purpose.** For meals, write the names of attendees and the business topic discussed on the receipt (or in a note linked to the receipt). "Lunch" is not a business purpose. "Lunch with Sarah Chen — discussed Q3 marketing contract" is.
- **Keep bank statements and credit card statements.** These provide corroborating evidence even when a receipt is lost.
- **Back up digital records.** Store copies of receipts in a cloud service (Google Drive, Dropbox, etc.) and locally. A house fire that destroys paper records does not excuse you from proving your deductions.

## Tax Record Retention Rules

How long do you need to keep tax documents? The answer depends on which document and what risk you're protecting against. The short version: keep your actual **tax returns forever** and keep your **supporting documents** for 3–7 years.

Here is the full breakdown:

| Document Type | How Long to Keep | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Filed tax returns (1040, etc.) | Forever | Proof of filing; needed for amended returns, Social Security, mortgage applications |
| W-2s and 1099s | 7 years | Supports the return; matches IRS document-matching window |
| Business expense receipts | 3–7 years | 3 years standard; 6 years if income underreported 25%+ |
| Charitable contribution records | 3 years from filing | Supports Schedule A deduction |
| Investment purchase records (cost basis) | Until you sell + 3 years | You need cost basis to calculate capital gains |
| Real property purchase records | Until you sell + 3 years | Needed to calculate gain on sale and home sale exclusion |
| Home improvement records | Until you sell + 3 years | Improvements increase your basis, reducing your gain |
| Employment tax records | 4 years | If you had employees |
| Records related to a loss carryforward | Until loss is used + 3 years | Carryforward losses link back to original loss year |

## Statute of Limitations IRS

The statute of limitations is the IRS's deadline for auditing you. Once it expires, the IRS generally cannot assess additional tax for that year. Understanding these windows is important for deciding how long to keep records and how much risk exposure you actually have.

The three key windows are:

- **3 years** — The standard statute of limitations. The IRS has 3 years from the later of the return filing date or the return due date (April 15) to audit your return and assess additional tax. This covers the vast majority of situations.
- **6 years** — If you underreported your gross income by more than 25%, the IRS gets 6 years. This is not about lying — it could be a legitimate misunderstanding about whether income was taxable. The threshold is gross income, not taxable income.
- **No limit** — If you filed a fraudulent return, or if you never filed a return at all, the statute of limitations never runs. There is no expiration date on fraud.

\[
\text{Standard Limitation Period} = \text{Filing Date (or Due Date, whichever is later)} + 3 \text{ years}
\]

!!! info "The Clock Starts When You File"
    If you file your 2024 return on February 10, 2025, the 3-year clock starts on April 15, 2025 (the due date) — not February 10. If you file late (say, on October 10, 2025, after an extension), the clock starts on October 10, 2025. Filing early does not shorten your exposure window; it starts on the due date at the earliest.

## IRS Notice CP2000

The CP2000 is one of the most misunderstood notices the IRS sends. **It is not an audit.** It is an automated notice generated by the IRS's document-matching system, which compares the income reported on your return to the 1099s, W-2s, and other information returns filed by payers. When there is a discrepancy, the IRS sends a CP2000 proposing a change to your return.

Common reasons you might receive a CP2000:

- You forgot to report a 1099-NEC from a client
- You received a 1099-B from selling stock but did not report it (or reported it differently)
- You received a 1099-INT or 1099-DIV that you missed
- A 1099 was issued in error or contains the wrong amount

The notice will show you the income the IRS believes you underreported, the additional tax it proposes, interest charges, and possibly a penalty. You have **60 days to respond**.

Your three options:

1. **Agree** — If the notice is correct, sign the response form and pay the additional amount (or set up a payment plan).
2. **Disagree** — If you already reported this income correctly, or the 1099 is wrong, write a response explaining your position and attach supporting documents (your return pages, original 1099, corrected 1099, etc.).
3. **Partially agree** — If part of the notice is correct and part is not, you can agree to the portion that is right and dispute the rest.

!!! example "CP2000 in Practice"
    Marcus did some freelance design work in 2024 and received a \$4,200 payment from a client. He reported the income on his Schedule C. However, the client's bookkeeper accidentally issued a 1099-NEC for \$42,000 — an extra zero. The IRS's CP2000 proposes that Marcus owes tax on an additional \$37,800 of income. Marcus responds with a copy of his bank statement showing the actual \$4,200 deposit, a letter explaining the discrepancy, and a request that the client issue a corrected 1099. The IRS reviews his response and closes the case with no additional tax owed.

Do not ignore a CP2000. Ignoring it converts the proposed change into an actual assessment, and then you owe the money — plus interest that has been accruing the entire time.

## Penalty Abatement Request

If you end up owing a penalty — for filing late, paying late, or failing to make estimated tax payments — you may not have to pay the full amount. The IRS offers a program called **First-Time Penalty Abatement (FTA)** that can eliminate certain penalties entirely.

**Who qualifies:**
- You have filed all required returns (or have a valid extension)
- You have paid, or arranged to pay, all taxes owed
- You have no penalties in the prior three tax years (clean compliance history)

**Which penalties FTA covers:**
- Failure-to-file penalty (5% of unpaid tax per month, up to 25%)
- Failure-to-pay penalty (0.5% of unpaid tax per month, up to 25%)
- Failure to deposit (for business payroll taxes)

**Which penalties FTA does NOT cover:**
- Estimated tax penalties (use Form 2210 or the reasonable cause exception instead)
- Accuracy-related penalties
- Fraud penalties

**How to request FTA:**

The easiest method is to call the IRS at 1-800-829-1040 and ask for penalty abatement under the First-Time Abatement policy. Have your tax year, the penalty amount, and your compliance history ready. The IRS representative can approve it on the phone for smaller amounts. For larger penalties, you may need to submit a written request. You can also request abatement after you receive an IRS bill for the penalty — you do not have to request it preemptively.

If you don't qualify for FTA because you had a prior penalty, you may still request abatement based on **reasonable cause** — circumstances beyond your control such as serious illness, a natural disaster, or the death of a family member. This requires a written explanation and is evaluated case by case.

## Offer in Compromise IRS

If you owe the IRS more than you can reasonably pay — not just "more than is comfortable," but genuinely more than you can pay given your income and assets — an **Offer in Compromise (OIC)** allows you to settle your tax debt for less than the full amount owed.

The IRS accepts an OIC when one of three conditions applies:

- **Doubt as to collectibility** — You don't have enough income or assets to pay the debt in full before the collection statute expires (typically 10 years from assessment). This is the most common basis.
- **Doubt as to liability** — There is genuine dispute about whether you actually owe the amount assessed.
- **Effective tax administration** — You could technically pay the full amount, but doing so would create severe economic hardship or be fundamentally unfair given exceptional circumstances.

**The OIC process:**

1. Check eligibility using the IRS's free OIC Pre-Qualifier tool at irs.gov before applying.
2. Complete **Form 656 (Offer in Compromise)** and **Form 433-A (OIC)** (Collection Information Statement).
3. Submit an application fee of **\$205** (waived if your income is at or below the federal poverty guidelines).
4. Choose a payment structure: lump sum (20% of offer amount upfront, balance within 5 months of acceptance) or periodic payment (pay in installments while the IRS considers your offer).
5. Wait. The IRS typically takes 6–12 months to evaluate an OIC. During this time, collection activity is suspended.

The IRS's acceptance rate for OICs is roughly **40%**. The offers that are accepted tend to be well-documented, realistic (not lowball offers with no financial basis), and submitted by people who genuinely cannot pay the full debt.

!!! warning "OIC Red Flags to Avoid"
    Be skeptical of companies advertising "settle your IRS debt for pennies on the dollar." While OICs are legitimate, many tax relief companies charge high upfront fees for applications that have little chance of approval. Before paying anyone, use the IRS's free Pre-Qualifier tool and consider a free consultation with a nonprofit credit counselor or a fee-only tax professional. You can file an OIC yourself — the IRS process is open to anyone.

The IRS calculates its **Reasonable Collection Potential (RCP)** to determine the minimum offer it will accept:

\[
\text{RCP} = \text{Net Realizable Value of Assets} + \text{Future Income} \times \text{Multiplier}
\]

Where the multiplier is 12 (for lump sum offers) or 24 (for periodic payment offers), and "future income" means your monthly disposable income after allowable living expenses. Your offer needs to at least equal the RCP.

## Key Takeaways

- The IRS audits about **0.4% of individual returns** — roughly 1 in 250. Most audits are correspondence audits resolved by mail.
- The three audit types — correspondence, office, and field — vary enormously in seriousness. Treat each appropriately.
- Common audit triggers include large charitable deductions, business losses multiple years in a row, cash-intensive businesses, unreported crypto, and missing 1099s.
- The home office deduction is legitimate if you meet the exclusive use test. The simplified method reduces scrutiny.
- If audited, respond to exactly what is asked — no more. Send copies, use certified mail, and consider professional representation for anything beyond a simple correspondence audit.
- Keep contemporaneous records: receipts over \$75, mileage logs, business purpose notes on meals, dedicated business accounts.
- Keep your actual **tax returns forever**; supporting documents for **3–7 years** depending on the situation.
- The standard statute of limitations for an IRS audit is **3 years** from the filing date (or due date, whichever is later). It extends to 6 years if you underreported income by 25%+, and there is no limit for fraud.
- A **CP2000 notice is not an audit** — it is a proposed change from the IRS's automated matching system. Respond within 60 days: agree, disagree, or partially agree.
- If you have a clean 3-year compliance history, **First-Time Penalty Abatement (FTA)** can eliminate failure-to-file and failure-to-pay penalties with a phone call.
- An **Offer in Compromise** lets you settle tax debt for less than owed if you genuinely can't pay. The acceptance rate is about 40%. Use the IRS's free Pre-Qualifier tool before applying.

## What To Do Right Now

1. **Locate your last three years of filed tax returns.** If you don't have copies, you can request a transcript at irs.gov/transcripts for free. File them somewhere permanent.
2. **Create a record-retention folder** (physical or digital) for the current tax year. Label it with the year and start dropping receipts, bank statements, and 1099s into it as they arrive.
3. **Set up a mileage tracking app** if you use your vehicle for business. The deduction is valuable; the log is required. Pick one app and use it consistently from day one of the tax year.
4. **Open a separate business checking account and credit card** if you are self-employed and don't already have one. This single step eliminates most record-keeping headaches.
5. **Review last year's return for the common triggers listed in this chapter.** Do your charitable deductions look unusual relative to your income? Do you have multiple years of Schedule C losses? If so, make sure your documentation is solid.
6. **If you received a CP2000 or other IRS notice and have not responded**, respond immediately. Call the IRS (1-800-829-1040) or work with a tax professional. Every day you wait, interest accrues.
7. **Check if you qualify for First-Time Penalty Abatement** if you have a penalty on an existing tax balance. Call 1-800-829-1040, ask for the Penalty Abatement department, and reference the FTA policy.
8. **If you have a tax debt you cannot pay**, visit the IRS OIC Pre-Qualifier at irs.gov/oic before paying any private company to evaluate your situation.
