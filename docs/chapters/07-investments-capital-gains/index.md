## The Core Idea: You're Taxed on the Profit, Not the Sale Price

When you sell an investment for more than you paid, the IRS wants a share of that profit. The profit itself is called a **capital gain**, and the tax you owe on it is the **capital gains tax**. Sounds simple enough — but the rate you pay depends heavily on *how long you held the investment* before selling. Get that timing wrong and you could pay two or three times as much tax as necessary.

The foundational formula is:

\[
\text{Capital Gain (or Loss)} = \text{Sale Proceeds} - \text{Cost Basis}
\]

Your **cost basis** is what you originally paid for the asset, including commissions and fees. If you bought 100 shares of a stock at \$40 per share, your cost basis is \$4,000. If you later sell those shares for \$6,000, you have a \$2,000 capital gain. Whether that \$2,000 is taxed at 10% or 23.8% depends on one critical factor: the holding period.

## Part 1: Short-Term vs. Long-Term Capital Gains

### Short-Term Capital Gains Tax

If you sell an asset you've held for **one year or less**, the gain is **short-term**. Short-term capital gains are taxed as ordinary income — meaning they stack on top of your wages and get taxed at the same marginal rate as your salary. In 2025, that could be anywhere from 10% to 37% depending on your total income.

This is the IRS's way of discouraging quick trading in favor of long-term investing. If you bought a stock in January and sold it in October of the same year with a \$5,000 profit, that \$5,000 gets added to your W-2 income and taxed accordingly. For someone in the 24% bracket, that's \$1,200 in tax. For someone in the 32% bracket, it's \$1,600.

### Long-Term Capital Gains Tax

Hold the asset for **more than one year** — meaning at least one year and one day — and you qualify for long-term capital gains treatment. These rates are substantially lower than ordinary income rates and are one of the most valuable tax preferences in the entire tax code for individual investors.

The holding period clock starts the day *after* you buy the asset and ends on the day you sell it. Buy on March 5, 2024; sell on March 6, 2025 — that's long-term. Sell on March 5, 2025 — that's still short-term (you need that one extra day).

### Capital Gains Tax Rates 2025

Here are the 2025 long-term capital gains tax brackets. These are separate from the ordinary income brackets and are applied only to your long-term gains and qualified dividends.

| Rate | Single Filers | Married Filing Jointly |
|------|--------------|----------------------|
| 0%   | Up to \$48,350 | Up to \$96,700 |
| 15%  | \$48,351 – \$533,400 | \$96,701 – \$600,050 |
| 20%  | Above \$533,400 | Above \$600,050 |

!!! tip "The 0% Rate Is Real — and Often Overlooked"
    If your total taxable income (including the gain) falls below the 0% threshold, you owe zero federal tax on long-term capital gains. A retired couple with \$60,000 in Social Security and pension income might still qualify for the 0% rate on gains if their taxable income is low enough. Run the numbers before assuming you owe anything.

In addition to the standard rates, high earners face a **Net Investment Income Tax (NIIT)** of 3.8% on the lesser of: (a) their net investment income, or (b) the amount by which their Modified Adjusted Gross Income (MAGI) exceeds \$200,000 (Single) or \$250,000 (MFJ). This means the effective top federal rate on capital gains can reach 23.8% (20% + 3.8%) for high-income taxpayers.

## Part 2: Capital Losses — When Investments Go Wrong

### Capital Loss Deduction Rules

Losses happen. When you sell an investment for less than your cost basis, you have a **capital loss**. The good news: the IRS lets you use those losses to offset your gains — and if your losses exceed your gains, you can even deduct up to **\$3,000 per year against ordinary income** (wages, salary, etc.).

The netting process works like this:

1. First, short-term gains and losses are netted against each other.
2. Then, long-term gains and losses are netted against each other.
3. If both nets are the same sign (both gains, or both losses), you simply report each.
4. If one is a net gain and the other is a net loss, they offset each other.
5. Any remaining net capital loss can offset up to \$3,000 of ordinary income.

!!! example "Capital Loss Offset in Action"
    You sold Stock A at a \$8,000 short-term gain, Stock B at a \$5,000 long-term gain, and Stock C at a \$15,000 long-term loss. After netting: your \$5,000 long-term gain is wiped out by part of the \$15,000 loss, leaving a \$10,000 net long-term loss. That wipes out the \$8,000 short-term gain entirely, leaving a \$2,000 net capital loss. You deduct \$2,000 against ordinary income this year. Zero carryforward.

### Capital Loss Carryforward

If your total net capital loss exceeds \$3,000 in a given year, you don't lose the excess. It **carries forward indefinitely** to future tax years. If you have a \$25,000 net capital loss this year, you use \$3,000 now and carry \$22,000 forward. Next year, that \$22,000 can offset future gains first, then up to another \$3,000 against ordinary income — and so on until the carryforward is exhausted.

The carryforward preserves its character: a long-term loss carryforward remains long-term, and a short-term loss carryforward remains short-term. This matters because long-term losses first offset long-term gains (at the preferential rate), while short-term losses first offset short-term gains (taxed as ordinary income).

## Part 3: The Wash Sale Rule

### Wash Sale Rule

The IRS figured out a long time ago that investors would love to sell a losing stock on December 31, claim the tax loss, and immediately repurchase the same stock on January 1 — maintaining their position while manufacturing a tax deduction. Congress closed this loophole with the **wash sale rule**.

A wash sale occurs when you sell a security at a loss *and* buy a "substantially identical" security within **30 days before or after the sale** — a 61-day window total. If you trigger the wash sale rule, the loss is **disallowed** for the current year. Instead, the disallowed loss is added to the cost basis of the replacement shares, so you don't lose the loss permanently — you just defer it.

!!! warning "Common Wash Sale Traps"
    - Selling a stock at a loss and buying it back within 30 days — obvious, but people still do it.
    - Selling a mutual fund and buying a nearly identical fund from the same fund family (if the IRS considers them "substantially identical," it's a wash sale).
    - Selling in a taxable account and buying the same security in an IRA within the 30-day window — this *does* trigger the wash sale, but the deferred loss is **permanently lost** because it can't be added to the IRA's basis.
    - Automatic dividend reinvestments that purchase shares within the 30-day window can create partial wash sales.

The fix is straightforward: wait 31 days before repurchasing the same security, or replace it with a *similar but not identical* investment. For example, if you sell an S&P 500 index fund at a loss, you can immediately buy a total market index fund and stay fully invested while preserving the loss deduction.

## Part 4: Dividends — Ordinary vs. Qualified

### Ordinary Dividends Tax Rate

When a corporation pays out profits to shareholders, those payments are called dividends. The default treatment is **ordinary dividends**, taxed at your regular marginal income tax rate — the same rates as your wages. Box 1a of your 1099-DIV reports your total ordinary dividends.

### Qualified Dividends Tax Rate

Dividends that meet certain requirements get preferential **qualified dividend** treatment — taxed at the same lower rates as long-term capital gains (0%, 15%, or 20%). To be "qualified," a dividend must be:

- Paid by a U.S. corporation or a qualified foreign corporation
- You must have held the underlying stock for **more than 60 days** during the 121-day period centered on the ex-dividend date

Box 1b of your 1099-DIV shows your qualified dividends (a subset of box 1a). Most dividends from large U.S. companies traded on major exchanges are qualified. REIT dividends, money market dividends, and some foreign company dividends are typically not qualified.

!!! note "Qualified Dividends vs. Ordinary: A Quick Dollar Comparison"
    Suppose you receive \$10,000 in dividends. If you're in the 22% ordinary income bracket and all dividends are ordinary, you owe \$2,200. If those same dividends are qualified and you're in the 15% long-term gains bracket, you owe \$1,500 — a \$700 difference on the same income. Over decades of investing, this adds up significantly.

## Part 5: Cryptocurrency and Digital Assets

### Cryptocurrency as Property (IRS Treatment)

Since IRS Notice 2014-21, the IRS has treated cryptocurrency as **property**, not currency. This single classification has enormous consequences for how crypto is taxed. Every time you sell, trade, or spend cryptocurrency, you have a **taxable event** — you must calculate gain or loss just as if you had sold stock.

This means:
- Selling Bitcoin for dollars: taxable event
- Trading Bitcoin for Ethereum: taxable event (you "sold" Bitcoin for its fair market value, then "bought" Ethereum)
- Buying a pizza with Bitcoin: taxable event (the IRS sees this as selling Bitcoin at its current value)
- Receiving crypto as payment for work: taxable as ordinary income at the fair market value when received
- Mining or staking rewards: generally taxable as ordinary income when received

The only events that are *not* taxable: transferring crypto between your own wallets, buying crypto with dollars (the purchase itself isn't taxable — but it starts your cost basis clock).

### Crypto Cost Basis Tracking

Because every transaction is a taxable event, tracking your cost basis across potentially thousands of transactions is one of the biggest practical challenges in crypto taxation. The IRS allows the same cost basis methods used for stocks (FIFO, specific identification), but crypto exchanges often don't provide the clean 1099-B reports that stock brokers do. You may need to use dedicated crypto tax software (CoinTracker, Koinly, TaxBit, etc.) that pulls data from exchange APIs and computes gains/losses automatically.

!!! warning "DeFi and Cross-Chain Complexity"
    If you've used decentralized exchanges (DEX), liquidity pools, or bridged assets across blockchains, your tax situation becomes significantly more complex. Each token swap on a DEX is a taxable sale. Providing liquidity and receiving LP tokens may be a taxable exchange. There is limited IRS guidance on some DeFi activities — consult a tax professional familiar with crypto if your situation involves these transactions.

### NFT Tax Treatment

Non-fungible tokens (NFTs) are taxed as **collectibles** by the IRS, not as ordinary capital assets. The practical difference: long-term capital gains on collectibles are taxed at a **maximum rate of 28%**, which is higher than the 20% maximum rate that applies to most long-term capital gains. If you bought an NFT, held it over a year, and sold it at a profit, you're looking at up to 28% federal tax on that gain — not 20%.

Short-term NFT gains are still taxed as ordinary income (the same as any short-term gain), and the NIIT may apply on top of the regular rate for high earners. If you create and sell NFTs as part of a business, the income is self-employment income, subject to both income tax and self-employment tax.

## Part 6: Cost Basis Methods

### Cost Basis Methods Overview

When you own multiple lots of the same security (bought at different times and prices) and sell some shares, you need to decide *which shares* you're selling. The IRS allows several methods, and the choice can have a dramatic impact on your tax bill.

### FIFO Cost Basis Method

**FIFO (First In, First Out)** is the IRS default. If you don't specify otherwise, the IRS assumes you sold your oldest shares first. This makes sense for record-keeping simplicity, but it's not always optimal — your oldest shares may have the lowest basis and the largest gains, and they may already be long-term while newer shares are still short-term.

!!! example "FIFO in Action"
    You bought 100 shares of XYZ at \$20 (January 2022), then 100 more at \$50 (June 2024). Now the stock is at \$60, and you sell 100 shares. Under FIFO, you sold the January 2022 shares: gain = \$60 - \$20 = \$40 per share × 100 = \$4,000 long-term gain. If you had sold the June 2024 shares instead, the gain would be only \$10 per share × 100 = \$1,000 — but short-term.

### Specific Identification Method

**Specific identification** lets you choose exactly which lot of shares you're selling, allowing precise tax optimization. You could select the shares with the highest cost basis (to minimize gain), or specifically choose long-term shares over short-term shares to get the preferential rate.

The catch: you must make the election **at the time of the sale**, not after the fact. You tell your broker (in writing or through the brokerage's online interface) which specific lot you're selling before the trade settles. Your broker must confirm the instruction. If you wait until tax time and then try to pick the lot, the IRS won't allow it.

The average cost method is available only for **mutual funds** — you take the total cost of all shares divided by the total number of shares to get a uniform per-share basis. Once elected for a fund, it applies to all future sales of that fund at that broker (with some exceptions).

| Method | Best For | Tax Optimization | Election Required |
|--------|----------|-----------------|-------------------|
| FIFO | Simplicity, default | Low (oldest shares first) | No |
| Specific ID | Maximum flexibility | High (choose any lot) | Yes, at time of sale |
| Average Cost | Mutual funds | Moderate | Yes (for mutual funds) |

## Part 7: Mutual Funds and ETFs

### Mutual Fund Capital Gains

Here's a tax quirk of mutual funds that surprises many investors: even if *you* don't sell any shares of your mutual fund, the fund itself buys and sells securities throughout the year. When those internal transactions generate net capital gains, the fund is required to distribute those gains to shareholders — and you owe tax on them even if you automatically reinvested the distribution.

These **capital gains distributions** are reported in box 2a of your 1099-DIV (total capital gain distributions) and must be reported on your return. The distributions retain their character — most are long-term gains at the preferential rate — but you still owe tax in the year of distribution regardless of your fund's overall performance. It's entirely possible to receive a capital gains distribution from a fund that lost money that year.

If you're investing in a taxable account (not a 401(k) or IRA), this is a meaningful disadvantage of actively managed mutual funds compared to their index ETF counterparts.

### ETF Tax Efficiency

**Exchange-Traded Funds (ETFs)** have a structural advantage over mutual funds when it comes to capital gains distributions. ETFs use an **in-kind creation and redemption** mechanism: large institutional investors (called "authorized participants") can create new ETF shares by delivering a basket of the underlying securities to the ETF, or redeem ETF shares by receiving those securities back — all without any cash changing hands inside the fund.

This means the ETF can shed low-basis shares by handing them out in-kind during redemptions, avoiding a taxable sale entirely. As a result, most broad market ETFs distribute little or no capital gains annually. You control when you realize gains (when you sell your ETF shares), which is far more tax-efficient for long-term investors in taxable accounts.

!!! info "ETF vs. Mutual Fund: Taxable Account Rule of Thumb"
    For money held in taxable (non-retirement) accounts, prefer index ETFs over equivalent mutual funds when possible. You'll get similar market exposure with significantly fewer unexpected tax bills from internal fund trading. In a 401(k) or IRA, this distinction matters much less since gains inside the account aren't currently taxable.

## Part 8: Municipal Bonds

### Municipal Bond Tax Exemption

Interest paid on **municipal bonds** (bonds issued by state and local governments to fund public projects) is exempt from **federal income tax**. This exemption is written directly into the tax code and makes municipal bonds particularly attractive to high-income investors.

Additionally, if you hold bonds issued by your own state (or a locality within your state), that interest is often also exempt from your **state income tax** — making it triple-tax-exempt if you're also exempt from local taxes. For an investor in the 37% federal bracket plus a 10% state bracket, a municipal bond paying 4% effectively yields the same after-tax return as a taxable bond paying over 7%.

The **tax-equivalent yield** formula lets you compare:

\[
\text{Tax-Equivalent Yield} = \frac{\text{Municipal Bond Yield}}{1 - \text{Marginal Tax Rate}}
\]

For example, a 3.5% muni yield for someone in the 32% federal bracket:

\[
\frac{0.035}{1 - 0.32} = \frac{0.035}{0.68} \approx 5.15\%
\]

That 3.5% muni is equivalent to a taxable bond yielding 5.15% for that investor.

!!! note "Munis Aren't Always Better"
    For investors in lower tax brackets, the lower pre-tax yield of municipal bonds may not compensate for the tax exemption. Do the math using your actual marginal rate. Also note: municipal bond interest from private-activity bonds may be subject to the Alternative Minimum Tax (AMT), and any capital gain from selling a muni at a profit is fully taxable.

## Part 9: Stock Options

### Stock Options Tax Overview

If your employer grants you stock options as part of your compensation, you're sitting on a potentially valuable benefit — and a genuinely complex tax situation. There are two fundamentally different types of stock options, and they're taxed in completely different ways. Mixing them up is a costly mistake.

### Incentive Stock Options (ISO)

**Incentive Stock Options (ISOs)** are the more tax-favored variety. The key points:

- **At grant**: No tax.
- **At exercise** (when you buy the shares at the strike price): No *regular* income tax. However, the "spread" (the difference between the fair market value and the strike price) is an **AMT preference item** and may trigger the Alternative Minimum Tax.
- **At sale**: If you hold the shares for at least **1 year after exercise** *and* at least **2 years after the grant date**, the entire gain gets long-term capital gains treatment — taxed at 0%, 15%, or 20%.
- If you sell before meeting those holding periods (a **disqualifying disposition**), the spread at exercise becomes ordinary income.

!!! warning "ISOs and the AMT: A Hidden Tax Surprise"
    Exercising ISOs late in the year in a year when the stock price has risen significantly can create a large AMT liability. The AMT spread is calculated on the difference between the exercise price and the fair market value at exercise — even if you haven't sold the shares yet. Some employees have exercised ISOs on stock that later crashed, leaving them with a large AMT bill and shares worth far less than the tax owed. If you're planning to exercise a large number of ISOs, model the AMT impact before year-end.

### Non-Qualified Stock Options (NSO)

**Non-Qualified Stock Options (NSOs)** — also called NQSOs or NQOs — are the more common type. The tax treatment is simpler but less favorable:

- **At grant**: No tax.
- **At exercise**: The spread (FMV at exercise minus the strike price) is taxed as **ordinary income** in the year of exercise. Your employer is required to withhold payroll taxes and income tax on this amount. Your W-2 will include it, though it may be buried in box 1.
- **Basis**: Your cost basis in the shares becomes the FMV at exercise (not the strike price), so you've already been taxed on the spread.
- **At sale**: Any additional gain or loss after exercise is a capital gain or loss, long-term or short-term depending on how long you hold the shares after exercise.

| Feature | ISO | NSO |
|---------|-----|-----|
| Tax at grant | None | None |
| Tax at exercise | None (but AMT possible) | Ordinary income on spread |
| Employer withholding | No | Yes |
| Sale tax treatment | LTCG if holding periods met | Capital gain/loss on post-exercise appreciation |
| Who can receive | Employees only | Employees, contractors, directors |

## Part 10: Tax-Loss Harvesting

### Tax-Loss Harvesting Timing

**Tax-loss harvesting** is the practice of strategically selling investments that are currently trading below your cost basis to realize a capital loss — then using that loss to offset capital gains (and up to \$3,000 of ordinary income). Done properly, it's one of the most powerful and accessible tax planning strategies available to individual investors.

The mechanics:

1. Identify holdings in your taxable accounts with unrealized losses.
2. Sell those positions to realize the loss.
3. Immediately reinvest the proceeds in a *similar but not identical* fund or security to stay invested in the market (avoiding the wash sale rule).
4. The realized loss offsets your gains this year; any excess carries forward.

The key is the **"similar but not identical"** replacement. You want to maintain your investment exposure and asset allocation, but you can't buy back the exact same security within 31 days. Common pairings that work: Vanguard Total Stock Market ETF (VTI) ↔ iShares Core S&P Total U.S. Stock Market ETF (ITOT). They track different indexes but have highly similar performance.

Tax-loss harvesting is most valuable when:

- You have short-term gains (taxed as ordinary income) — offsetting those with losses saves the most per dollar of loss
- You're in a high tax bracket
- You have significant unrealized losses but want to stay invested in the market
- It's late in the year and you can see your full-year gain picture

It's least valuable (or irrelevant) when:

- All your investments are inside tax-advantaged accounts (401k, IRA) — no taxable gains to harvest against
- Your income is low enough that you're already in the 0% LTCG bracket
- The replacement investment you'd buy has materially different risk/return characteristics than what you're selling

## Part 11: Investment Tax Forms

### 1099-DIV Explained

The **1099-DIV** is the form your brokerage or mutual fund company sends you (and the IRS) when you've received dividends or capital gain distributions during the year. You need this form to fill out your Schedule B and ultimately your Form 1040.

Key boxes to know:

| Box | What It Reports | Tax Treatment |
|-----|----------------|---------------|
| 1a  | Total ordinary dividends | Ordinary income rates |
| 1b  | Qualified dividends | LTCG rates (0%/15%/20%) |
| 2a  | Total capital gain distributions | Generally LTCG rates |
| 2b  | Unrecaptured Section 1250 gain | 25% maximum rate |
| 2d  | Collectible gains | 28% maximum rate |
| 3   | Nondividend distributions (return of capital) | Reduces cost basis; not currently taxable |
| 4   | Federal income tax withheld | Direct credit against your tax bill |

Box 1b is always a subset of box 1a — qualified dividends are a portion of ordinary dividends that happen to qualify for the lower rate. When you enter your 1099-DIV in tax software, it handles the rate difference automatically.

### 1099-B Explained

The **1099-B** is the primary form for reporting capital gains and losses. Your broker sends one for every year in which you had any sales of securities. It reports each individual transaction with:

- **Proceeds** (what you received from the sale)
- **Cost basis** (what you paid, if the broker has it)
- **Holding period** (short-term or long-term)
- Whether basis was reported to the IRS (**covered** securities) or not (**noncovered** securities)

The **covered/noncovered** distinction matters. For **covered** securities (stocks purchased after January 1, 2011; mutual funds and ETFs after January 1, 2012), the broker is required to report your cost basis to the IRS on the 1099-B. For **noncovered** securities (bought before those dates, or certain other situations), the broker reports proceeds only, and you must provide basis from your own records.

!!! warning "Don't Let the IRS Calculate Your Basis"
    If you have noncovered securities and file without providing your cost basis, the IRS will treat your basis as zero — meaning you'll owe tax on the full sale proceeds. Always track your basis for noncovered positions. Old brokerage statements, personal records, or transfer documents are acceptable.

Your 1099-B feeds directly into **Schedule D** (Capital Gains and Losses) and Form 8949 (Sales and Other Dispositions of Capital Assets). Most tax software imports your 1099-B electronically and populates these automatically.

### Schedule B Interest and Dividends

**Schedule B** is a supplemental form that you attach to your Form 1040 when:

- Your total taxable interest income exceeds **\$1,500**, or
- Your total ordinary dividend income exceeds **\$1,500**, or
- You had a **financial interest in or signature authority over a foreign financial account**, or
- You received income from a foreign trust

Part I of Schedule B lists each payer of interest income and the amounts. Part II does the same for dividends. Part III asks the foreign account and trust questions — even if your total is under \$1,500, you still need Schedule B if you had foreign accounts (and potentially FBAR filing obligations, which are separate from your tax return).

If all your interest and dividends total under \$1,500 and you had no foreign accounts, you simply enter the totals directly on Form 1040 without Schedule B.

## Putting It All Together: A Year-End Investment Tax Checklist

By mid-November each year, you have enough information to make smart moves before December 31. Here's how the concepts in this chapter connect into action:

- **Review unrealized gains and losses** in all taxable accounts. Identify candidates for tax-loss harvesting.
- **Check your holding periods.** If you're close to one year on a winning position, consider waiting the few extra days to convert a short-term gain to long-term.
- **Match gains and losses.** If you've already realized gains this year, selling losers can offset those gains dollar-for-dollar.
- **Watch the wash sale window.** If you harvested losses in early December, remember the 30-day clock — don't repurchase the same security before January.
- **Check for mutual fund distributions.** Many funds distribute capital gains in November or December. If you're considering buying a mutual fund in a taxable account late in the year, check if it's about to make a large distribution — you might be buying a tax bill.
- **Review your stock options.** If you have ISOs, model the AMT impact before exercising. If you have NSOs, understand that exercising creates ordinary income in that year.
- **Calculate your estimated LTCG rate.** Use the 2025 brackets to see if any of your gains will fall in the 0% zone — which changes your harvesting priorities.

## Key Takeaways

- **Holding period is everything.** More than one year and one day qualifies for long-term capital gains rates (0%, 15%, or 20%), which are far lower than ordinary income rates that apply to short-term gains.
- **Capital losses are valuable.** They offset gains dollar-for-dollar, and up to \$3,000 per year can offset ordinary income. Excess losses carry forward indefinitely.
- **The wash sale rule is strict but navigable.** Don't repurchase substantially identical securities within 31 days of selling at a loss — but you can immediately replace with similar (not identical) investments.
- **Qualified dividends get the same preferential rates as long-term capital gains.** Ordinary dividends do not. Check box 1b vs. box 1a on your 1099-DIV.
- **Crypto is property, not currency.** Every sale, trade, or spending transaction is a taxable event requiring gain/loss calculation.
- **NFTs are collectibles** and face a 28% maximum long-term rate — higher than standard LTCG rates.
- **Your cost basis method matters.** FIFO is the default; specific identification gives you control to minimize taxes, but you must elect it at the time of sale.
- **ETFs are more tax-efficient than mutual funds** in taxable accounts due to the in-kind redemption mechanism that minimizes capital gains distributions.
- **Municipal bond interest is federal-tax-free** and often state-tax-free if issued in your state — use the tax-equivalent yield formula to compare against taxable bonds.
- **ISOs offer long-term capital gains treatment** if holding period requirements are met, but can trigger AMT at exercise. NSOs create ordinary income at exercise — no holding period work-around.
- **Tax-loss harvesting** is a practical year-round (not just year-end) strategy to reduce your tax bill without abandoning your investment strategy.
- **1099-DIV, 1099-B, and Schedule B** are the three primary forms for investment income — understand what each box means so you can spot errors and enter data correctly.

## What To Do Right Now

1. **Log in to your brokerage account** and look for a "tax center" or "cost basis" section. Confirm your cost basis method election (FIFO vs. specific identification) before your next sale.
2. **Download last year's 1099-DIV and 1099-B** and find boxes 1a, 1b, 2a. Compare: how much of your dividend income was qualified vs. ordinary? Did you have any capital gain distributions from mutual funds?
3. **Review your holdings for unrealized gains and losses.** If you have significant unrealized losses, note them — you may want to harvest them before year-end.
4. **Check your holding periods.** For any position you're thinking of selling that's close to the one-year mark, calculate the exact date you need to wait to qualify for long-term treatment.
5. **If you own cryptocurrency**, tally your transactions from the past year. If you used multiple exchanges, consider connecting them to crypto tax software before you're scrambling in April.
6. **If you have employer stock options**, identify whether they are ISOs or NSOs (check your option grant agreement or ask HR). Model the tax impact of exercising before doing so.
7. **Calculate your tax-equivalent yield** on any bonds or CDs you're evaluating. If you're in the 24% bracket or higher, municipal bonds deserve a serious look for your taxable accounts.
8. **If your interest + dividends may exceed \$1,500**, set a reminder to attach Schedule B to your return and verify you haven't had any foreign financial accounts to report.
