## State Income Tax Overview

Your federal tax return is just the beginning. Every year, most Americans file a second (or third, or fourth) return — to their state. State income taxes are collected separately from the IRS by each state's own revenue department, and the rules, rates, and forms are entirely their own. Federal and state taxes are independent systems that happen to share some of the same underlying numbers.

Here's how the two systems connect: your state return typically starts with your federal Adjusted Gross Income (AGI), then makes its own additions and subtractions to arrive at your **state taxable income**. From there, the state applies its own tax rates, credits, and deductions. The result — what you owe or what gets refunded — is completely separate from your federal tax bill.

Think of it this way: the federal government sets the foundation, and your state builds its own house on top of it. Some states build a modest ranch; California builds a mansion with a 13.3% top rate.

!!! info "The SALT Cap — Where Federal and State Taxes Collide"
    The Tax Cuts and Jobs Act of 2017 capped the federal deduction for **State and Local Taxes (SALT)** at **$10,000** per return (the same cap applies whether you're single or married filing jointly). This means if you pay $8,000 in state income taxes and $5,000 in property taxes, you can only deduct $10,000 on your federal Schedule A — not $13,000. High-tax states like New York, New Jersey, and California lobbied hard against this cap because it effectively raised the federal tax burden for their residents.

## No-Income-Tax States List

Nine states impose no broad-based individual income tax. If you live in one of these states and your income comes from wages, you're in luck — no state income tax return to file.

| State | No Tax On | Watch Out For |
|---|---|---|
| Alaska | All income | No state income tax; no sales tax either |
| Florida | All income | Property taxes are significant in many counties |
| Nevada | All income | Sales tax up to 8.375% in some counties |
| New Hampshire | Wages & salaries | Investment income (dividends/interest) still taxed |
| South Dakota | All income | No state income tax |
| Tennessee | Wages & salaries | Investment income still taxed (Hall Tax repealed 2021) |
| Texas | All income | Property taxes are among the highest in the nation |
| Washington | All income | **7% capital gains tax** on gains over $262,000/year |
| Wyoming | All income | No state income tax or capital gains tax |

!!! warning "New Hampshire and Tennessee Are Not Fully Tax-Free"
    As of 2025, both NH and TN tax **investment income** — specifically dividends and interest — so if you have a large investment portfolio, don't assume you'll escape all state tax just by living there. New Hampshire's interest and dividends tax is being phased out and is scheduled for full repeal, but verify the current status before filing.

!!! warning "Washington State's Capital Gains Tax"
    Washington has no income tax, but passed a **7% capital gains tax** on long-term gains exceeding **$262,000** per year. This caught many tech workers and investors off guard when it took effect. The tax applies to gains from stocks, bonds, and other capital assets — but notably **excludes real estate**. If you sold company stock and your gains topped $262,000, you owe Washington state money even though there's "no income tax."

## Multi-State Filing Rules

Life doesn't stay inside one state's borders. If you earned income in more than one state during the year, you generally have to file a return in each state where you earned that income. The rules break down into three filing statuses that states use:

- **Resident return**: Filed in your home state (your state of domicile or statutory residence) on all your worldwide income.
- **Nonresident return**: Filed in a state where you earned income but don't live. You're only taxed on the income earned within that state.
- **Part-year resident return**: Filed in a state where you lived for part of the year (for example, if you moved mid-year). You're taxed on all income earned while you were a resident, plus any income from in-state sources during the nonresident portion.

The practical math: you'll often pay tax to both states on the same income. To prevent true double-taxation, your home state typically offers a **credit for taxes paid to other states**. The credit is usually the lesser of (a) what you actually paid to the other state, or (b) what your home state would have charged on that same income. You don't get a windfall — you pay the higher of the two rates — but you don't pay both states in full.

\[
\text{Credit for taxes paid to another state} = \min\!\left(\text{Tax paid to other state},\ \frac{\text{Out-of-state income}}{\text{Total income}} \times \text{Home state tax}\right)
\]

!!! example "Multi-State Filing: Marcus Works in Two States"
    Marcus lives in New Jersey and commutes into New York City four days a week; he works remotely from home (NJ) one day a week. His total salary is $120,000. New York will tax the 80% he earned there ($96,000); New Jersey will tax his full $120,000 as a resident, then give him a credit for the tax he paid to New York on that $96,000. He files two returns: a NY nonresident return and an NJ resident return.

## State Domicile vs. Residency

These two words sound interchangeable, but to tax authorities they are very different — and confusing the two can cost you a lot of money.

**Domicile** is your permanent legal home — the place you intend to return to whenever you're away. You can only have one domicile at a time. It doesn't change just because you spend a lot of time somewhere else. If you grew up in Ohio, still have your driver's license there, and your parents' house is your "permanent address," Ohio may argue that's still your domicile even if you've been renting an apartment in California for three years.

**Statutory residency** is different. Most states define you as a resident — for tax purposes — if you spend **183 or more days** in that state during the year, regardless of where your domicile is. This is sometimes called the "183-day rule," and it can trigger **dual residency**: two states each claiming you as a full resident and each taxing your worldwide income. The credit for taxes paid to other states reduces the sting, but it doesn't always eliminate it entirely, especially when the two states have different rates, different deductions, or different treatment of specific income types.

Factors courts and auditors look at when determining domicile:

- Where is your driver's license and vehicle registered?
- Where are you registered to vote?
- Where do you keep your primary bank accounts and safe deposit box?
- Where is your primary doctor, dentist, and attorney?
- Where do your children attend school?
- Where do you spend the most time, and where do you spend the holidays?
- Where do you list as your home address on legal documents, passports, and tax returns?

!!! tip "The Domicile Test Is About Intent, Not Just Days"
    Spending 200 days in Florida doesn't automatically make you a Florida domiciliary if your "closest connections" are still in New York. Conversely, you can establish Florida domicile even if you spend some time in New York — as long as you've taken affirmative steps to change your domicile and your facts support it. Document everything: the date you got your Florida license, updated your voter registration, opened a Florida bank account, and transferred your medical records.

## State Tax Reciprocity

Here's a welcome simplification for millions of commuters: **reciprocity agreements** between neighboring states allow you to pay income tax only to your home state, even if you work across the border. Without reciprocity, you'd file two returns and claim a credit; with it, you file only one.

Common reciprocity agreements (2025):

| States in Agreement | How It Works |
|---|---|
| Pennsylvania / New Jersey | PA residents working in NJ (and vice versa) pay only to their home state |
| Maryland / DC / Virginia | Residents of any one who work in another pay only to their home state |
| Indiana and IL, IA, KY, MI, WI | Indiana residents working in those states (and vice versa) file only at home |
| Virginia / DC / MD / PA / WV / KY / NC | VA residents working in these states stay on their VA return |
| Wisconsin / IL, IN, KY, MI | Same cross-border simplification |

The mechanics: if you live in New Jersey and work in Pennsylvania, give your employer a **Pennsylvania Certificate of Nonresidence** (Form REV-419). Your employer stops withholding Pennsylvania tax from your paycheck and withholds New Jersey tax instead. At tax time, you file only a New Jersey return. Clean and simple.

!!! warning "Reciprocity Only Covers Wages and Salaries"
    Reciprocity agreements generally apply only to **employee compensation** — wages, salaries, tips. If you have other income from the neighboring state (rental income, self-employment income from clients there, etc.), reciprocity doesn't help. You'll still need to file a nonresident return in that state for that other income.

## Remote Worker State Tax Rules

Remote work scrambled the traditional rule that you pay state taxes where your employer is located. The modern default rule is simpler: **you owe state income tax to the state where you actually perform your work.** If you live and work from home in Colorado, Colorado gets the tax — even if your employer's HQ is in New York.

But there's a major exception: the **"convenience of the employer" doctrine**, applied by a handful of states. Under this rule, if you work remotely for a reason that benefits *you* (convenience) rather than your employer (necessity), the state where your employer is based can tax your income as if you worked there in person.

States that apply the convenience doctrine (as of 2025):

- New York
- New Jersey
- Pennsylvania
- Connecticut
- Delaware
- Arkansas

!!! warning "New York's Convenience Rule Is Aggressively Enforced"
    If your employer is headquartered in New York, New York will tax your remote income unless you can demonstrate that your remote work location is a **necessity of the employer** — for example, the employer requires it because they have no suitable office for you, or your role requires proximity to clients outside New York. Simply preferring to work from home in Texas does not satisfy the necessity test. New York has audited thousands of remote workers who stopped commuting to NYC offices during and after the pandemic.

## Moving to a No-Tax State

Moving to a state with no income tax sounds like a slam dunk. And it can be — but only if you actually change your domicile in the eyes of the law. States (especially California and New York) have revenue departments that actively audit former residents who claim to have moved but still maintain significant ties to the old state.

The steps to establish a new domicile are not complicated, but they must be deliberate and documented:

1. **Get a driver's license** in the new state and surrender your old one.
2. **Register to vote** in the new state.
3. **Update your address** on bank accounts, brokerage accounts, credit cards, and your passport.
4. **Transfer your medical records** to a doctor in the new state.
5. **Execute a new will or update your estate documents** listing your new state as your domicile.
6. **Physically move** — count your days carefully. Keep a diary or use a calendar app with location data if you travel a lot.
7. **Notify your former state** of your change of address on your final part-year return.

California is particularly aggressive. If you earn income from California sources after moving (rental property in CA, CA clients for your consulting business, stock options that vested while you were a California employee), California may still have a claim on that income. They even have **Form 3840** specifically for allocating deferred compensation income earned in California but paid out after you've moved away.

!!! tip "The 'Closest Connections' Test"
    Courts resolve domicile disputes using a totality-of-facts test sometimes called the "closest connections" test. No single factor is decisive. A person who spends 200 days in Florida but keeps their New York apartment, their New York doctor, their New York country club membership, and sends their kids to New York schools will likely lose a New York domicile audit. The goal is for your *life* to genuinely reflect your new home — not just your tax strategy.

## State-Specific Deductions

Once you've determined your home state and allocated income correctly, you need to figure out what deductions your state actually allows. This is where significant differences emerge. States diverge from the federal tax code in three common ways:

**Things federal allows that states often don't:**

- The $10,000 SALT deduction (states don't deduplicate this — you're not deducting your own state's taxes on the state return)
- The federal Qualified Business Income (QBI) deduction (Section 199A) — many states don't allow it
- Mortgage interest deduction phase-outs — some states have stricter limits
- Student loan interest deduction — some states have their own version; others don't

**Things states allow that federal doesn't:**

- Deductions for contributions to that state's 529 college savings plan (very common — usually $5,000–$10,000 per contributor)
- Retirement income exclusions — many states fully or partially exempt Social Security, military pensions, and government pensions from state tax
- Property tax "circuit breaker" credits for seniors and low-income homeowners
- Renter's credits — some states offer a credit based on rent paid as a proxy for property tax

!!! note "529 Deductions Are State-Specific"
    If you contribute to your state's own 529 plan, you usually get a state income tax deduction. But if you contribute to *another state's* 529 plan (even if it has better investment options), you typically lose the state deduction. A few states (including Pennsylvania, Arizona, Kansas, and Missouri) allow deductions for contributions to any 529 plan, regardless of state. Check your state's rules before choosing a 529.

## State Standard Deduction

Every state that has an income tax makes its own choice about standard deductions. Some are generous; some are minimal; a handful of states have no standard deduction at all and only allow itemized deductions.

A sample comparison for Tax Year 2025:

| State | Single Standard Deduction | Married Filing Jointly |
|---|---|---|
| Federal | $14,600 | $29,200 |
| California | $5,202 | $10,404 |
| New York | $8,000 | $16,050 |
| Illinois | None (itemized only) | None (itemized only) |
| Pennsylvania | None (flat rate on gross income) | None |
| Colorado | Conforms to federal ($14,600) | Conforms to federal ($29,200) |
| Texas | No income tax | No income tax |
| New Jersey | $1,000 | $2,000 |
| Oregon | $2,745 | $5,495 |
| Georgia | $5,400 | $7,100 |

Notice that states like New Jersey and Oregon offer standard deductions that are a small fraction of the federal amount. This means that even if you take the federal standard deduction, you may end up itemizing on your state return (or having a much smaller deduction), which increases your state taxable income — and your state tax bill.

Pennsylvania operates differently from most: it uses a flat rate applied to **gross income** with very limited deductions at all, so the concept of a "standard deduction" doesn't really apply in the same way.

## Conformity to Federal Tax Law

When Congress changes the federal tax code — passes a new law, creates a new deduction, modifies a credit — states don't automatically update their own tax laws. How quickly (or whether) a state adopts those changes depends on its **conformity approach**:

**Rolling conformity**: The state automatically adopts whatever the federal tax code says as of the current date. If Congress passes a new law in October, rolling conformity states apply it immediately. Examples: New York, California (with some exceptions), and most states.

**Static conformity**: The state's tax code is frozen as of a specific date. Any federal changes after that date don't apply unless the state legislature acts separately to update the conformity date. Example: Virginia's conformity date is updated periodically but is not automatic.

**Selective conformity**: The state picks and chooses which federal provisions to adopt, creating a patchwork. Some federal deductions are adopted; others are explicitly rejected. Example: California selectively conforms — it adopted most federal changes but does not conform to the federal QBI deduction, the increased bonus depreciation rules, or certain TCJA provisions.

!!! info "Why Conformity Matters for Your Return"
    If your state uses static conformity and its conformity date precedes a federal change that benefited you (say, a new above-the-line deduction), you can't take that deduction on your state return. This requires an **addition** to your state taxable income — a line where you add back the federal deduction that your state doesn't recognize. Tax software handles this automatically for most common differences, but if you're preparing your return manually or have unusual items, check your state's conformity status.

## State Estimated Tax Payments

If you have income that isn't subject to withholding — freelance income, investment income, rental income, business income — you're responsible for paying your state estimated taxes quarterly, just as you do federally. Most states mirror the federal system closely, with a few important differences.

The general rule: if you expect to owe more than a certain threshold (typically $500 to $1,000, depending on the state) after withholding, you should make quarterly estimated payments to avoid a penalty.

Typical state estimated tax due dates (most states follow federal):

- **Q1**: April 15
- **Q2**: June 15
- **Q3**: September 15
- **Q4**: January 15 of the following year

!!! warning "States Don't Always Match Federal Due Dates"
    Some states have moved their own estimated tax due dates to differ slightly from federal. California, for example, front-loads payments: **30% is due April 15, 40% is due June 15, 0% in September, and 30% is due January 15.** This is the opposite of the equal-quarterly approach at the federal level and catches many California taxpayers off guard. Always verify your state's specific schedule.

To calculate what you owe each quarter, states generally use the same safe harbor rules as the IRS: pay either 100% of last year's state tax liability (110% if your income is high), or 90% of the current year's actual liability. Meet either threshold and you avoid underpayment penalties even if you end up owing more at filing.

\[
\text{Quarterly state estimated payment} = \frac{\text{Prior year state tax}}{4} \quad \text{(safe harbor method)}
\]

Most states allow online payment through their revenue department's website, and many accept credit cards (though often with a convenience fee). Some states also allow you to set up automatic debits for your quarterly payments, similar to IRS Direct Pay.

## State Tax Refund Taxability

Here's a scenario many people overlook: you received a state income tax refund last year. Is that refund taxable income on your federal return this year? The answer is: **it depends on whether you itemized.**

The logic: if you deducted your state income taxes on last year's federal Schedule A (itemized deductions), you got a tax benefit from that payment. When the state sends some of it back, you have to report the refund as income — because you essentially over-deducted. You benefited from a deduction you didn't fully "earn."

On the other hand, if you took the standard deduction last year, you never deducted your state taxes at all. The refund is simply the state returning your own money — no federal tax benefit was taken, so no federal tax is owed on the refund.

The IRS formalized this with the **tax benefit rule**: you only include the state refund in income to the extent you actually received a tax benefit from the deduction. This is reported on **Schedule 1, Line 1** of your federal Form 1040 as "Taxable refunds, credits, or offsets of state and local income taxes."

!!! example "State Refund Taxability: Two Scenarios"
    **Scenario A — Itemizer:** Last year, Priya itemized her deductions and deducted $9,000 in California state taxes on her federal return. This year, California sent her a $1,200 refund (she overpaid). That $1,200 is taxable income on her current-year federal return because she got a full deduction for the $9,000 last year.

    **Scenario B — Standard Deduction:** Last year, David took the standard deduction ($14,600) on his federal return, so he deducted nothing for his state taxes. This year, Ohio sent him a $600 refund. David does **not** report the $600 as income — he never deducted those state taxes, so there's no benefit to claw back.

One more wrinkle: the tax benefit rule limits taxability. If deducting your state taxes didn't actually *reduce* your federal tax (because, say, AMT applied or the SALT cap limited your deduction to $10,000 anyway), then the refund attributable to the non-deducted portion is not taxable. This gets complicated quickly, but tax software walks you through the worksheet automatically.

## Key Takeaways

- State income taxes are a completely separate system from federal taxes, but they typically start with your federal AGI as a baseline and make their own adjustments.
- Nine states have no broad-based income tax, but "no income tax" doesn't always mean zero state tax obligation — watch for capital gains taxes (Washington), investment income taxes (New Hampshire), and high property taxes (Texas).
- If you earn income in more than one state, you generally file a resident return in your home state and nonresident returns in each other state. Your home state typically gives you a credit for taxes paid to other states.
- Domicile (your permanent legal home) and statutory residency (183+ days in a state) are different concepts. Getting both wrong can result in two states taxing your full worldwide income.
- Reciprocity agreements between neighboring states let you pay income tax only to your home state on wages — use them by filing a nonresidence certificate with your employer.
- Remote workers owe income tax to the state where they actually work — except in states (NY, NJ, PA, CT, DE, AR) that apply the "convenience of the employer" doctrine, which can tax remote workers as if they worked in the employer's state.
- Moving to a no-tax state requires real steps: new driver's license, voter registration, updated bank accounts, changed medical records, and a genuine shift in your center of life. Keep records.
- States often differ sharply from federal law on deductions — some don't allow the QBI deduction, some offer 529 plan deductions, and state standard deductions are usually much lower than federal.
- States use rolling, static, or selective conformity to adopt federal tax law changes; know your state's approach so you don't miss required additions or subtractions.
- If you have self-employment, freelance, or investment income, you likely owe quarterly estimated taxes to your state — due dates and safe harbor percentages vary by state.
- If you itemized last year and received a state tax refund, that refund may be taxable income on your current federal return under the tax benefit rule.

## What To Do Right Now

1. **Look up your state's income tax rate** at your state's department of revenue website. If you live in a flat-tax state (Pennsylvania at 3.07%, Illinois at 4.95%, Colorado at 4.4%), a simple calculation tells you roughly what you owe. If your state has brackets, find where your income falls.
2. **Check whether you need to file in more than one state.** Review your W-2s for Box 15 (state) and Box 16 (state wages). If you see two different states listed — or two W-2s from the same employer with different state withholding — you likely need to file a nonresident return.
3. **Verify your domicile.** If you've recently moved, or if you split time between states, audit your own facts: Where is your driver's license? Where are you registered to vote? Where is your primary doctor? If the answer isn't clearly your intended home state, take the steps to correct it now — before a state auditor does the analysis for you.
4. **Check for a reciprocity agreement** if you commute across state lines for work. Search "[your state] reciprocity agreement [neighboring state]" — if one exists, submit a nonresidence certificate to your employer to stop unnecessary withholding in the non-home state.
5. **Remote workers: find out your employer's HQ state.** If it's in New York, New Jersey, Pennsylvania, Connecticut, Delaware, or Arkansas, research whether the convenience of the employer doctrine applies to your situation. Talk to a tax professional if your remote work arrangement is complex.
6. **Find your state's standard deduction.** Compare it to your itemized deductions on your state return — especially if you pay significant mortgage interest or make charitable contributions. You may itemize on your state return even when you take the federal standard deduction.
7. **Look up your state's 529 contribution deduction** if you have children or are saving for education. If your state offers a deduction for its own 529 plan, contributing before December 31 could meaningfully reduce your state tax bill.
8. **Calculate whether you need to make state estimated tax payments.** If you freelance, have rental income, or significant investment income not subject to withholding, log in to your state's revenue portal and set up quarterly payments. Underpayment penalties at the state level are real and avoidable.
9. **Retrieve last year's state tax refund amount** from your records (it's on the state refund check or direct deposit, or on the prior-year state return). If you itemized last year, compare that refund to what you reported on last year's Schedule 1 — you may need to include it in income this year.
10. **If you moved this year**, prepare to file part-year returns in both states. Gather documentation of your exact move date, your new address confirmation, and any income earned in each state during each residency period.
