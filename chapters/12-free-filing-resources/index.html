    Introduction: You Don't Have to Pay to File¶

    Here's a fact that surprises a lot of people: the IRS has negotiated deals, built its own tools, and funded volunteer networks — all so that most Americans can file their federal return for free. The tax software industry has worked hard to obscure this, but the options are real, well-supported, and used by millions of people every year.

    This chapter is your map. We'll walk through every major free filing option, give you an honest comparison, and then shift gears to a topic that's just as important: knowing when "free" isn't the right answer. Not every tax situation belongs in self-service software. Some situations — a new S-Corp, a divorce, an inheritance, rental properties — call for a licensed professional, and paying for that expertise is a smart investment, not a sign of failure.

    By the end of this chapter, you'll know exactly where to go to file for free if your situation qualifies, what warning signs mean you need a professional, how to check your IRS records online any time, and how to give someone else official permission to deal with the IRS on your behalf. Let's finish strong.

    The IRS Free File Program¶

    The IRS Free File Program is a formal partnership between the IRS and a coalition of private tax software companies. Under the agreement, these companies offer free federal tax preparation and e-filing to taxpayers whose Adjusted Gross Income (AGI) falls at or below $84,000 for the 2025 tax year. That income threshold covers roughly 70% of all US taxpayers — so there's a good chance you qualify.

    The key detail that most people miss: you must access Free File through IRS.gov/freefile. If you go directly to a software company's website and navigate their menus, you will almost certainly land on a paid product. The only way to guarantee you're getting the free federally-negotiated version is to start at the IRS portal. From there, you'll see a list of participating partners — currently eight software companies — and each one has its own eligibility criteria beyond the AGI cap (some have age requirements, some restrict certain states, some require simple returns).

    IRS Free File: Quick Facts

    • Income limit: AGI ≤ $84,000
    • Access: IRS.gov/freefile — do NOT go directly to a software company's site
    • Federal filing: Always free
    • State filing: May cost extra depending on the partner
    • Partners: 8 software companies as of 2025
    • Best for: W-2 income, standard or itemized deductions, common credits (Earned Income Credit, Child Tax Credit)

    Free File guided software works like a standard interview — it asks questions, you answer, and it builds your return behind the scenes. If your return is straightforward, this is genuinely a great option. If you have self-employment income, rental income, or complex investments, you may find yourself hitting the limits of what the free version supports and being pushed toward a paid upgrade.

    IRS Direct File: The IRS's Own Tool¶

    Direct File is different from Free File in an important way: it is built and operated directly by the IRS, not a private company. There are no upsells, no partner agreements, and no data being shared with third parties. The IRS launched Direct File as a pilot and has been expanding it; for 2025, it is available in approximately 25 states.

    The tradeoff is scope. Direct File is designed for people with relatively simple tax situations. It handles W-2 income, Social Security benefits, unemployment compensation, HSA deductions, the standard deduction, and a selection of common tax credits. It does not support self-employment income, investment income beyond basic interest, or itemized deductions in their full complexity. If your return fits within those boundaries and you live in a participating state, Direct File is a clean, IRS-native experience with no commercial pressure attached.

    Is Your State Participating in Direct File?

    Direct File handles only your federal return. To check whether your state is supported and whether a state filing integration is available, visit IRS.gov/directfile. The participating state list expands each year — if your state wasn't included last year, check again.

    VITA: Free In-Person Help From Certified Volunteers¶

    VITA stands for Volunteer Income Tax Assistance, and it is one of the IRS's most underutilized programs. VITA sites are staffed by IRS-certified volunteers — real people who have passed IRS training and passed competency exams — who prepare your return in person, for free.

    VITA is available to three groups:

    • Taxpayers with income generally at or below $67,000
    • Persons with disabilities
    • Taxpayers with limited English proficiency

    Finding a site is straightforward: go to IRS.gov/vita or call 211 (United Way's helpline). Sites are typically open January through April and are hosted in community centers, libraries, schools, and churches. You bring your documents — Social Security cards, W-2s, 1099s, prior year return, photo ID — and a volunteer prepares the return while you watch. You review it, sign it, and it gets e-filed on the spot.

    What to Bring to a VITA Site

    • Photo ID (and spouse's if filing jointly)
    • Social Security cards or ITIN letters for all household members
    • All W-2s, 1099s, and other income documents
    • Bank account and routing number for direct deposit
    • Prior year's tax return (if available — helps verify AGI)
    • Records of deductible expenses (childcare costs, student loan interest, HSA contributions)

    The quality at VITA sites is consistently good. Volunteers are not guessing — they follow IRS training and use IRS-approved software. For many working families, VITA is the single best free filing option available.

    AARP Tax-Aide: Free for Everyone¶

    AARP Tax-Aide is similar to VITA in that it offers free, in-person tax preparation by trained volunteers — but it has no income limit and no age requirement, despite the name. AARP Foundation operates it, but you do not need to be an AARP member or be over 50 to use it. Anyone can walk in.

    Tax-Aide operates more than 3,500 locations nationwide and is especially well-suited for retirees navigating pension income, Social Security taxation, and Required Minimum Distributions — areas where volunteers receive specific training. You can find a location at aarpfoundation.org/taxaide.

    The practical difference between VITA and Tax-Aide comes down to location availability in your area. In some communities, you'll find one but not the other. Both are excellent; use whichever has a convenient location for you.

    Free Tax Software: A Side-by-Side Comparison¶

    If you want to file yourself from your computer or phone, you have several genuinely free options beyond the IRS programs. Here's an honest comparison:

    Software Federal State Best For
    IRS Free File (via partner) Free May cost extra AGI ≤ $84,000; interview-based guided filing
    IRS Direct File Free Free (in participating states) Simple W-2 / SS / unemployment returns; ~25 states
    Cash App Taxes Free Free Most return types; truly no upsell
    FreeTaxUSA Free $14.99 Broad coverage including Sch C, Sch D, rentals
    H&R Block Free Online Free Free (simple) W-2, standard deduction, basic credits
    TaxAct Free Free Varies Basic returns; limited credit support
    TurboTax Free Edition Free* Varies Very simple returns only — see warning below

    Cash App Taxes (formerly Credit Karma Tax) deserves a special mention because it is genuinely free for both federal and state — no income limits, no credit card required, no upsells. It supports a surprisingly wide range of forms including Schedule C (self-employment), Schedule D (capital gains), and rental income. The interface is less polished than TurboTax, but the price is unbeatable.

    FreeTaxUSA charges $14.99 for state filing but nothing for federal, and it handles more complex situations than most free options — including self-employment, investment income, and rental properties. For a taxpayer who needs Schedule C and wants to avoid a $100+ software bill, FreeTaxUSA is often the right call.

    The TurboTax Free Edition Warning¶

    TurboTax deserves its own section because it is aggressively marketed and widely misunderstood. There are two different free TurboTax products, and the distinction matters enormously:

    TurboTax Free Edition (what you find at turbotax.com) is a commercial product that only handles the simplest possible returns: W-2 income, standard deduction, and a narrow set of credits. If you have student loan interest, HSA contributions, 1099-NEC income of any kind, or almost any deduction beyond the standard, TurboTax will tell you mid-return that you need to upgrade — and that upgrade typically costs $89–$169.

    TurboTax Free File is the version accessed via IRS.gov/freefile. It is part of the federal Free File agreement and covers a meaningfully broader range of situations for taxpayers under the $84,000 AGI threshold.

    TurboTax Free Edition Is Not the Same as Free File

    If you go directly to turbotax.com and select "Free Edition," you are NOT on the IRS Free File program. This is the commercial product that upsells most users. To access the actual free version under the IRS agreement, you must start at IRS.gov/freefile and follow the link from there. The IRS.gov/freefile portal will route you to the correct product.

    The practical advice: if you are looking for a completely free TurboTax experience, start at IRS.gov/freefile. If you're willing to pay a modest fee for a polished interface, TurboTax is a capable product — you're just paying for the experience, not something you couldn't otherwise do yourself.

    When to Hire a CPA or Enrolled Agent¶

    Free filing is the right move for most simple situations. But "simple" is doing a lot of work in that sentence. There are specific circumstances where paying for professional help is not just convenient — it is genuinely worth hundreds or thousands of dollars in tax savings, penalty avoidance, or protection if things go sideways.

    Consider hiring a licensed tax professional if any of the following apply to your situation:

    • You own a business — especially an S-Corp or multi-member LLC. Payroll, self-employment tax, basis tracking, and the Qualified Business Income deduction have enough complexity that DIY mistakes are common and expensive.
    • You have rental properties — depreciation schedules, passive activity loss rules, cost segregation, and eventual gain calculations at sale require precision.
    • A major life event occurred — divorce (dividing retirement accounts requires a QDRO; alimony rules changed in 2019), inheritance (basis step-up rules, estate tax thresholds), or exercising stock options (ISOs vs. NSOs have very different tax treatments).
    • You're significantly off on estimated taxes — if you're self-employed or have investment income and your quarterly payments were off by more than a few hundred dollars, a professional can help you avoid underpayment penalties and plan ahead.
    • You file in multiple states — apportionment rules, reciprocity agreements, and sourcing rules for income are genuinely complex and vary by state.
    • You received an IRS notice or are under audit — this is not a DIY situation. A licensed professional can represent you; software cannot.
    • You have complex investments — cryptocurrency, restricted stock units, options, wash sales, or foreign accounts (FBAR/FATCA requirements carry serious penalties for non-compliance).

    The cost of a CPA or Enrolled Agent for a moderately complex return typically runs $300–$800. For an S-Corp with a personal return, expect $800–$2,000+. That sounds like a lot until you realize that a single missed deduction, an incorrect basis calculation, or a missed S-Corp election can cost far more.

    Tax Professional Types: Who Does What¶

    Not everyone who prepares taxes has the same credentials or authority. Here's what the different titles mean — and why it matters:

    Title Credential Who Issues It Can Represent Before IRS? Best For
    CPA Certified Public Accountant State board of accountancy Yes (with Form 2848) Broad tax and accounting expertise; business owners
    EA Enrolled Agent IRS Yes, unlimited Specialized in taxes; audits, back taxes, IRS disputes
    Tax Attorney JD + Tax LL.M. (often) State bar Yes Legal tax matters, tax court, criminal tax issues
    PTIN Holder Preparer Tax ID Number only IRS No Basic return preparation only; no formal credential
    "Ghost Preparer" None — No Avoid completely — they won't sign your return

    The single most important thing to know: never use a preparer who refuses to sign your return. This is called a "ghost preparer," and it is a major red flag. The preparer is required by law to sign and include their PTIN on every return they prepare. If they won't sign, they know the return has problems — and you'll be the one who faces penalties if the IRS comes knocking.

    CPAs are the most versatile option: they can handle everything from personal returns to complex business structures, audits, and financial planning. Enrolled Agents specialize exclusively in taxes and are often the best choice for IRS disputes, back tax resolution, and taxpayers with straightforward businesses. An EA who focuses on taxes full-time often knows the tax code better than a generalist CPA.

    CPA vs. EA: Which Should You Hire?

    Suppose you have a Schedule C sole proprietorship that generated $85,000 in net profit. You received an IRS CP2000 notice (proposed changes to your return) and you're not sure if the IRS is right.

    Best fit: Enrolled Agent. The EA is IRS-licensed, specializes in exactly this kind of dispute, and can represent you in front of the IRS on both the CP2000 response and the underlying Schedule C issues. A CPA would also be appropriate — the key is to find someone with audit response experience, not just return preparation experience.

    IRS Power of Attorney: Letting Someone Speak for You¶

    When you hire a CPA or EA to represent you before the IRS — whether for an audit, a payment plan negotiation, or just to respond to a notice — you need to give them formal legal authority to do so. That authority comes through Form 2848: Power of Attorney and Declaration of Representative.

    Form 2848 is not complicated, but it is specific. You must:

    1. Identify yourself (name, address, SSN or EIN)
    2. Name your representative (name, address, credential/designation, PTIN or CAF number)
    3. Specify the tax matters covered — for example: "Form 1040, income tax, years 2023 and 2024"
    4. Specify what acts the representative is authorized to perform — receiving notices, making agreements, executing waivers, etc.
    5. Sign and date the form

    The IRS then registers the representative in the Centralized Authorization File (CAF) system, and going forward, when the IRS contacts you about the specified matters, a copy will also go to your representative. The representative can call the IRS on your behalf, attend hearings, and enter into installment agreements without you being present.

    Form 2848 Is Limited in Scope

    A Power of Attorney under Form 2848 only covers the specific tax periods and tax types you list. If you authorize someone for "Form 1040, 2023 and 2024" and then an issue arises for 2022, they cannot act on that year without a new Form 2848. Be specific when filling it out, and update it if your situation changes.

    You can submit Form 2848 by mail, fax, or — increasingly — through the IRS's online Tax Pro Account system. Once submitted and processed (usually a few business days for online submission), your representative can immediately begin working with the IRS on your behalf.

    Your IRS Online Account: A Tool You Should Be Using¶

    One of the most underused tools in personal tax management is the IRS Online Account, available at IRS.gov/account. Once you create an account (identity verification is handled through ID.me), you have access to a comprehensive dashboard of your tax records.

    Here is what you can see and do from your IRS Online Account:

    • View tax records and transcripts — account transcripts, return transcripts, wage and income transcripts, and record of account transcripts going back several years. These are the exact records the IRS has on file for you.
    • Check your prior year AGI — if you need last year's AGI to e-file this year's return and you can't find your copy, the IRS Online Account has it.
    • View payment history — see every estimated tax payment you've made, including dates and amounts.
    • Check your balance due — if you owe, you can see the current amount including penalties and interest.
    • Set up a payment plan — you can apply for an installment agreement online if you owe and can't pay in full right now.
    • View notices — some IRS notices now appear in your online account before the paper copy arrives.
    • Manage identity protection — you can request an IP PIN (Identity Protection PIN) to prevent someone from filing a fraudulent return in your name.

    Pull Your Wage and Income Transcript Before You File

    The Wage and Income Transcript shows every W-2, 1099, and information return that employers, banks, and brokers have reported to the IRS under your SSN. Pull this transcript before you file to make sure your return matches the IRS's records. If a 1099-B from your broker doesn't match what you report, the IRS's automated matching system will catch it and send you a notice. Catching discrepancies before you file is far easier than explaining them afterward.

    The identity verification step through ID.me requires uploading a photo ID and taking a selfie. It takes about 10–15 minutes the first time. It is worth doing now, before you are in a stressful situation (audit, balance due, identity theft issue) and need to access your records urgently. Create your account today at IRS.gov/account.

    Key Takeaways¶

    • The IRS Free File Program offers free federal filing to anyone with AGI ≤ $84,000 — but you must access it through IRS.gov/freefile, not directly through a software company's website.
    • IRS Direct File is the IRS's own free tool, available in ~25 states, with no commercial upsells — but it only handles simple returns (W-2, SS, unemployment, standard deduction).
    • VITA (IRS.gov/vita) provides free in-person filing by certified volunteers for taxpayers with income up to ~$67,000, people with disabilities, and limited English speakers.
    • AARP Tax-Aide (aarpfoundation.org/taxaide) offers free filing for anyone — no age requirement, no income cap — at 3,500+ locations nationwide.
    • Among self-service free software, Cash App Taxes is the most broadly free (federal + state, no income limits), and FreeTaxUSA is excellent for complex returns at free federal / $14.99 state.
    • TurboTax Free Edition at turbotax.com is not the same as IRS Free File — it upsells aggressively and covers only the simplest returns. Use IRS.gov/freefile to access the actual free version.
    • Hire a CPA or EA when you have business income, rental properties, major life events, multi-state filing, IRS notices, or complex investments. The cost is worth it.
    • Enrolled Agents specialize exclusively in taxes and are often the best choice for IRS disputes. CPAs offer broader expertise. Never use a preparer who won't sign your return.
    • Form 2848 (IRS Power of Attorney) gives a licensed professional formal authority to represent you before the IRS — for specific tax years and issues you list on the form.
    • IRS Online Account (IRS.gov/account) gives you access to your transcripts, prior year AGI, payment history, balance due, and payment plan options. Create your account now — before you need it urgently.

    What To Do Right Now¶

    1. Determine your AGI from last year's return (Line 11 of Form 1040). If it is at or below $84,000, you qualify for IRS Free File.
    2. Check your state at IRS.gov/directfile to see if Direct File is available to you. If it is and your return is simple, this is your easiest path.
    3. Look up the nearest VITA or AARP Tax-Aide site if you prefer in-person help or feel uncertain about filing on your own. Go to IRS.gov/vita or aarpfoundation.org/taxaide.
    4. Create your IRS Online Account at IRS.gov/account right now. Pull your Wage and Income Transcript and verify it matches the documents you have on hand.
    5. Decide on your filing method: Free File partner, Direct File, VITA, AARP Tax-Aide, or free self-service software. Use the comparison table and MicroSim in this chapter to match your situation to the right option.
    6. Assess whether you need a professional. Review the "When to Hire a CPA" list in this chapter. If two or more items apply to your situation, get a consultation — most CPAs and EAs offer a free initial call.
    7. If you hire a professional, prepare Form 2848 in advance so they can represent you before the IRS if needed. Have your SSN, their PTIN/CAF number, and the specific tax years and forms ready.
    8. File by April 15 — or file Form 4868 for an automatic six-month extension. Remember, an extension to file is not an extension to pay. If you owe, pay your estimate by April 15 to avoid penalties and interest.
